## 1. Allison J. Pugh’sLonging and Belonging: Parents, Children, and Consumer Culture

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Spring 2010
**Issue:** Emotional Control
**Section:** Book Reviews
**Word Count:** 1278
**Relevance Score:** 0.781

### Summary

The article discusses Allison J. Pugh's book *Longing and Belonging: Parents, Children, and Consumer Culture*, focusing on how children's consumer practices are tied less to individual wants than to social belonging and collective status. It describes Pugh's ethnographic study in Oakland, California, across three settings--upper-middle-class schools, private and public, and a low-income after-school program--where children create peer group systems Pugh calls "economies of dignity." These economies involve meaningful "scrip" (material items and experiences) along with norms for managing talk and negotiating whose purchases or activities count. The article emphasizes children's agency through "facework" strategies such as counterframing, bridging labor, claiming, concealing, and patrolling, enabling children to participate in peer culture even when they lack valued possessions. Examples include reframing thanks to family or ancestors instead of gifts, or shifting claims about Pokémon cards to explanations about saving for college. It then summarizes Pugh's analysis of parents, arguing that both wealthy and low-income families understand consumerism as connected to belonging, but respond differently by social class. Wealthier parents worry about media and hedonism and use allowances and rules, while lower-income parents focus more on affordability, using planning and saving. The article also highlights "pathway consumption," where families invest in activities and opportunities shaping children's future, illustrating social reproduction and inequality, alongside rare negative cases like families refusing consumption seen as wasteful.

## 2. Bobos in Purgatory

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** 2020
**Word Count:** 2350
**Relevance Score:** 0.755

### Summary

The article is a critical review of David Brooks's book *Bobos in Paradise: The New Upper Class and How They Got There*, which argues that a new American elite has emerged by blending bourgeois respectability with bohemian style. The reviewer discusses Brooks's "comic sociology," praising his observational accounts of how this class consumes, works, and presents itself--especially through upscale shopping districts, "Latte Towns," and lifestyle brands. A major theme is that Bobos transform conspicuous consumption into something that feels artistic, philosophical, ethical, or spiritually meaningful. Examples include packaged food and tea marketed with pseudo-profound messages, along with Brooks's "rules" of virtuous spending that frame luxuries as tools and restrict "vulgar" excess. The review also covers Brooks's broader sociological claims, including the idea that the educated class rose through meritocracy measured by credentials and testing ("Résumé Gods"), and the notion that the sixties were largely a meritocratic revolution. The reviewer challenges this, arguing that true meritocracy requires moral wisdom and equitable schooling, and that public education failures undermine equal opportunity. Finally, the article examines Brooks's account of "intellectual life" and criticizes its depiction as market-driven intellectual entrepreneurship. It contrasts commodified thought with the enduring, ideally independent role of universities, and concludes that politics and higher public service require deeper moral and educational foundations than the Bobo ethos provides.

## 3. The Historical Production (and Consumption) of Unsustainability

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Summer 2012
**Issue:** Sustain-Ability?
**Section:** Sustain-ability?
**Word Count:** 6245
**Relevance Score:** 0.720

### Summary

The article argues that today's environmental unsustainability is not only the result of visible harms (pollution, emissions, waste) but the outcome of historical cultural and institutional imperatives that actively reproduce unsustainable ways of living. It frames unsustainability as structurally "kept going" by three linked commitments: control, production, and consumption. First, it traces how industrial societies pursued control and order through standardization, quantification, and the reduction of complex natural processes into measurable quantities. This shift created physical and social distance between producers and the land, enabling large-scale extraction of resources and diminishing awareness of ecological limits. Railroads and standard time exemplify how technology embedded a worldview of overcoming "organic" constraints while relying on continuous external fuel and raw material inputs. Second, it describes the productionist paradigm, especially in agriculture and food policy, where maximizing output became an unquestioned goal. Quantified concepts like the calorie supported production targets in U.S. policy and foreign aid, reinforcing limitless agricultural intensification despite recurring problems like surplus and environmental costs. Third, it examines the consumerist imperative, where mass consumption became equated with progress and civic development. Government and business promoted consumption as identity and policy aim, even in the face of health and ecological constraints. The conclusion emphasizes that sustainability efforts must address these deeper, historically formed value systems; otherwise they only manage symptoms while leaving the underlying drivers intact.

## 4. Commodification and Consumer Society

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Summer 2003
**Issue:** The Commodification of Everything
**Section:** Bibliographic Review
**Word Count:** 3792
**Relevance Score:** 0.719

### Summary

The article is a bibliographic review of scholarly work on **commodification** and **consumer society**. It frames commodification as a longstanding concern, but one that has intensified alongside modern consumerism and major political-economic changes, especially the shift toward free markets in former communist states and the expansion of market logic into new social domains. It explains that commodification scholarship is broad, with competing assessments: some see commercialization as enabling freedom, wealth, creativity, and efficiency, while others warn about social inequality, exploitation, corruption of values, and environmentally damaging consumption patterns. The review outlines the literature in stages. It first surveys **classic theorists**, largely tied to Marxist and related traditions that emphasize class relations, production, alienation, and the "fetishization" of commodities, as well as status and prestige in consumption (Weber, Veblen). It then covers **contemporary approaches** that shift attention toward consumption itself--especially the symbolic meaning of goods--along with theories linked to postmodernism (notably Baudrillard's simulacra). Next, it traces **historical origins of consumer culture**, including earlier precedents in preindustrial and early modern periods, and the role of U.S. **postwar affluence**. It also reviews research on **advertising and shopping**, then addresses **ethical frameworks** for evaluating commodification, and concludes with both **celebrations of free markets** and **recent critiques** involving branding, overspending, emotional labor, and new luxury-driven consumption.

## 5. O imitators, you slavish herd!

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Summer 2020
**Issue:** Questioning the Quantified Life
**Section:** Book Reviews
**Word Count:** 1174
**Relevance Score:** 0.684

### Summary

The article is a review and critique of Robert H. Frank's book *Under the Influence*, which examines how social environments shape individual behavior, often in harmful ways. It frames the book as an account of "herd" dynamics--how peer pressure and social imitation spread choices like a contagion. The reviewer connects these ideas to major modern crises, arguing that social influence helps explain patterns ranging from climate-damaging consumption and wasteful energy use to the opioid epidemic and the persistence of consumerist lifestyles. The discussion highlights the book's emphasis on behavioral contagion: people adopt habits and products because others do, leading to escalating acquisitions such as larger electronics and greater connectivity, sometimes producing unintended consequences like isolation. The article also centers on climate change policy proposals discussed in the book, including progressive or consumption-based taxation, carbon taxes, and government-led interventions (such as large-scale investment in green technology and broader state action) rather than relying on voluntary individual restraint. Throughout, the reviewer describes how these social mechanisms interact with inequality and questions whether proposed policies would reduce harm without disproportionately burdening lower-income or elderly people, whose consumption may exceed their income in retirement.
