# The Historical Production (and Consumption) of Unsustainability

*Technology, Policy, and Culture*

## Introduction

All unsustainable activities resemble one another; each sustainable practice is sustainable in its own way.11xI wish to thank Wyatt Galusky, Laura Kolar, and Josh Yates for their feedback and discussion about earlier drafts of this essay. Those activities that cannot be sustained, alas, all succumb to the laws of thermodynamics and the very fact of organic life. They are alike in their need for resources and for inputs beyond themselves. They are not self-perpetuating. As for the sustainable options, whether they are ecological, economic, cultural, political, organizational, or moral, we have no shortage of ways to conceive of thinking in terms of _keeping something going_.

There is also no shortage of informed analysis of the problems of unsustainable activity. For the most part, these are commentaries on environmental health, from ecosystem integrity and public health to biodiversity, climate change, pollution, waste, and blight. A laundry list of metrics allows for these commentaries, revealing not only that the human draw from and impact on the nonhuman environment is increasing, but also that over the past century and a half the rate of increase is increasing. As Joshua Yates shows in his essay in this issue, those metrics range from GDP, carbon emissions, energy demands, and fossil fuel extraction to fertilizer use, water consumption, deforestation, and species extinction. While world population has more than tripled in the past century, water use has increased about fivefold, fertilizer consumption several hundredfold, forest and woodlands losses close to sixfold, and species extinction about twentyfold. The numbers are startling. Global expectations for how humans live on earth are insatiable.

But why are we in this position? And how does knowing how we got here inform plans, both large-scale and local, to aim for a more sustainable future?

With other essays in this issue working to explore and confound the premises and possibilities of sustainability, I want to look to the underside of the same issues by focusing on the historical production of unsustainability: how we have become so adept at keeping unsustainability going. My concern is that it is not just the visible material circumstances and the quantitative accounting of their consequences that we should attend to—too big a footprint, too many cars, too much carbon dioxide, too much waste—but the set of assumptions and cultural imperatives that make those patterns possible. It is the conceptual commitment to a set of relationships between human and nonhuman natures that deserves our attention, not merely those features that appear to us in the present and on the surface.

The problems of unsustainability are cultural as well as biological, exacerbated by human decisions about living in nature that often remain buried in the present debate and, thus, remain unquestioned. It took concerted efforts to build the infrastructure into our cultural and environmental landscapes that is now difficult to sustain, an infrastructure of modernity that draws from nature’s resources, distances humans from the land, operates without a sense of limits, and follows from a reduction of ecological realities—above and beyond the scope and scale of humanity—to anthropocentric constructs. This coterie of distance, limitlessness, extraction, and anthropocentrism adds up to produce a world always outpacing our capacity to keep something going.

In the Western industrial age of the past two centuries, industrialists, policy-makers, and everyday citizens alike built dominant forms of manufacturing with the technical values of control and order; promoted policies to foster production and focus on the values of efficiency and productivity—what in agricultural history has been called the “productionist paradigm”; and defined cultural ideals around consumer identity and consumerist values. Taken together, the three imperatives of control, production, and consumption define a historical process of concatenation, one that builds from the conceptual apparatus of control (through abstraction and quantification) to public policy (encouraging overproduction and increasing consumption) to full-blown identity construction (as with the modern consumer society).

My point is to proffer a necessary step in the challenge to ingrained patterns of thought and deeply entrenched structures of politics, economics, and technology—or, at the very least, to argue that this challenge will have to be launched if we want to truly become sustainable. By examining the historical momentum of this powerful set of cultural logics, we can see its contingency, rather than its inevitability. As imperatives or, if you prefer, ideologies, they do not just cause problems—they _are_ the problems. I review them in turn.

## The Control Imperative

Things weren’t yet used to being measured. Three stones and three leaves were not yet the same number, fifteen grams of peas and fifteen grams of earth not yet the same weight.... He had cut trails which grew back again as soon as he turned his back, felled trees which resprouted the next night, and yet, little by little, with stiff-necked determination, he had forcibly imposed a web of numbers over reluctant nature.22xDaniel Kehlmann, Measuring the World (new York: Pantheon, 2006) 97.

Questions of sustainable and unsustainable activity are bound up in the history of technology and industrialization. In particular, they are tied to the values of control and order that industrialists have long sought. Historians of technology write of the importance of key developments that helped secure this control, including the standardization, interchangeability, and uniformity that defined the rising industrial order of the nineteenth century.33x david Hounshell, From the American System to Mass Production, 1800â€“1932: The Development of Manufacturing Technology in the United States (Baltimore: Johns Hopkins University Press, 1985). Manufacturing facilities for Colt’s revolvers, Singer’s sewing machines, and any number of builders’ bicycles, farm implements, and meatpacking equipment exemplified the pattern of increasing control. The manufacturing systems chart out a business history from craft to industry, from cottage to factory, from guild to union, and from individual working identities to a hired system of labor and wages. As cultural achievements, these systems were successes in mechanization and organization. By the early twentieth century, they were dominant.

The technologically enabled industrial order also charted out a particular environmental history, since it was likewise an achievement in taking command of nature and a change in how one sought to control the natural world. The environmental dimension also reveals the difficulties that follow from fidelity to a control paradigm, a point sociologist Wolfgang Schivelbusch postulates as a cultural axiom of technology and risk: “there is an exact ratio between the level of technology with which nature is controlled, and the degree of severity of its accidents.”44xWolfgang Schivelbusch, The Railway Journey: The Industrialization and Perception of Time and Space (Berkeley: university of California Press, 1987) 131. The more we seek to control nature, the more risk we create. The industrial developments of the past few centuries were also environmental reconfigurations between humans and the non-human world. That is, we have to notice that our ability to sustain environmental integrity—to avoid the accident of environmental demise— runs in tension with industrial systems that work against that integrity.

For one, the new environmental configurations put space between producers and consumers, structuring relationships between people and the land premised on distance and separation—the move from craft to industry, from country to city, from cottage to factory. Industrial landscapes fostered the new configurations by establishing patterns of living that separated home and work and by developing new routes for raw material traffic: the food, energy, and water that had before been products of the home space now came from external, distant locations. Those configurations also followed from a mandate to extract resources from the earth and relied on commandeering nature, as with water-driven power systems that harnessed and then undermined the health of watersheds or new steam-powered engines that drew from the underground resource of coal or, later still, a reliance on oil and gas. The steel for weapons and ammunitions, the cotton that was made into thread, the soil that was tended with ever-greater speed and strength, the complete subordination of animal life to human manipulation—these were all accomplishments of harnessing and controlling nature.

The new relationships in the industrial age between human and nonhuman nature also required industrialists to commit to building systems that had no limits. Manufacturers relied on efficiency, productivity, control, uniformity, standardization, and power as guiding design principles for their pursuits. Those principles are limitless, however.55xSee Leo Marx, â€œdoes Improved Technology Mean Progress?,â€ Technology Review (January 1987): 33â€“41. Ruth Schwartz Cowanâ€™s work on the fallacy that labor-saving devices save time makes much the same point with respect to the gendered and domestic dimensions of efficiency and productivity. In short, labor-saving devices provide more time to do more labor. See Cowan, More Work for Mother: The Ironies of Household Technology from the Open Hearth to the Microwave (new York: Basic, 1983). one’s goal for more efficiency leads to a new goal for even more efficiency. Thus, the new order structured relationships between people and their natural environments in ways that relied on a set of anthropocentric values that had no end.

But how did this happen? What did it take to promote such developments? In the interests of space, let me point to one set of related features: the drive for quantification and the correlated reduction of nature to numbers. Broadly speaking, historians have done well to explore the reasons for the slow transitions across the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries from the qualitative to quantitative and what that transition meant for measuring people, places, and things.66xTore FrÃ¤ngsmyr, J. L. Heilbron, and Robin Rider, eds., The Quantifying Spirit in the Eighteenth Century (Berkeley: university of California Press, 1990); Theodore M. Porter, Trust in Numbers: The Pursuit of Objectivity in Science and Public Life (Princeton: Princeton university Press, 1995); James Scott, Seeing Like a State: How Certain Schemes to Improve the Human Condition Have Failed (New Haven: Yale University Press, 1998). Philosopher Max Horkheimer, for one, looked to that transition for its dual human and nonhuman consequences, noting, “domination of nature involves domination of man.”77xMax Horkheimer, The Eclipse of Reason (New York: Continuum, 2004) 64. To achieve that control (and, for the Frankfurt School, domination), leaders and planners argued for shifts in Western governance that encouraged quantification in systems of tax collecting, in naming conventions for individuals and families, in agricultural markets, in forest management, and even in defining the dirt beneath our feet.88xBenjamin R. Cohen, Notes from the Ground: Science, Soil and Society in the American Countryside (New Haven: Yale University Press, 2009).

A sample of dirt, for instance, could be understood—and had been understood— for its texture, color, compactness, surface appearance, even taste. Those variables were central to local farming cultures and were aimed at gauging soil quality based on experience and work. newer analytical methods of the nineteenth century, though, replaced these hands-on qualitative methods with quantified measures (such as chemical content, density, and the amounts of specific elements). dirt redefined as quantitatively analyzed soil stood in contrast to qualitative, judgment-based strictures that drew on local knowledge to define the land. Redefining dirt in this way also provided a necessary basis for legislating environmental activity—here agricultural activity—on large scales and across regions. Witness the u.S. department of Agriculture (USDA), a federal agency founded in 1862 on the premise that one could organize knowledge of the land based on quantified environmental concepts.

Quantification helps suggest how and why new numerical ways of thinking came about for the purpose of control and order, but of course the modern industrial era was made most visible through physical technological systems that depended on these conceptualizations of nature as something to be controlled. The train, for example, illustrates how new concepts about nature informed ways of materially building tech- nologies to control nature.

Railroads overcame the perceived constraints of nature, offering a solution to the problem of uninhibited or, at least, uncontrolled growth that comes with organic life. Trains need not be hampered by rainy weather, hot or cold days, dark nights, icy passages, or muddy roads. nor need they rely on the organic limits of animals that have to be fed, rested, and paced. At the same time, locomotives draw from fuel inputs (coal or wood) outside the system, requiring constant extraction and dependence on mined resources. Steel over water, coal over grain, machine over animal, the annihilation of space and time.

The railroad did no less than mimic Joshua’s stopping of the sun when its officers created standard time. Where “high noon” was inescapably defined as a measure following nature, standard time was defined as a measure of control and as a measure from man.99xWilliam Cronon, Natureâ€™s Metropolis: Chicago and the Great West (new York: norton, 1991).

Industrial triumphs were not a process of inevitable technological development. Governmental, business, and cultural decision-makers prioritized certain values over others in its construction. This is the phenomenon daniel Kehlmann points to in his novel _Measuring the World_ —“things were not yet used to being measured”—suggesting a lack of inevitability in his character’s travails working amidst unruly forest brush. Lewis Mumford captured much the same motivation and its consequences for human identity some time ago, noting that “in time-keeping, in trading, in fighting, men counted numbers; and finally, as the habit grew, only numbers counted.”1010xLewis Mumford, Technics and Civilization (New York: Harcourt, Brace, 1934) 22. Prussian foresters, antebellum soil-analyzing agrarians, French grain traders, and the Midwest’s vanguard meatpackers all illustrate the corollary “dangers of dismembering an exceptionally complex and poorly understood set of relations and processes in order to isolate a single element of instrumental value.”1111xScott 21.

A simplified, quantified vision of nature provided essential underpinnings for a control paradigm that defined modern society. That control ethos required devotion to constant extraction of resources from the earth, to the increased space between people and the land, and to goals that had no limits. In building modern industrial infrastructure, it required devotion, in other words, to a host of activities that were not sustainable on their own. Those activities remain with us, buried in our conceptual and physical infrastructures amidst widespread calls for sustainability. They not only lead to new problems that derive from illusory assumptions of control; they are the problem itself.

## _The Productionist Imperative_

It matters little whether the products are agricultural commodities or academic books and papers. The norms of productionism are… deeply rooted.1212xPaul Thompson, The Spirit of the Soil: Agriculture and Environmental Ethics (New York: Routledge, 1995)144.

Questions of unsustainable activity are likewise bound up in the history of production-oriented policies. With control and quantification as necessary underpinnings, the cherished value of productivity provided anchors for orienting human activity in nature in ways that led to, in today’s language, a continuously growing “footprint.” Industrialists had built the basic principles for this production-centered economy by the nineteenth century—control and quantification being two of them. But it was advocates of the early twentieth century who witnessed the culminating force of a dominant ideology of progress based on those principles and values. Those aspiring to produce more and more added support to an infrastructure requiring constant inputs, evermore energy, and a fundamentally unsustainable commitment to, as it were, taking a straw and drinking up the earth.

The zeal for production and its associated industrial value, productivity, touched all corners of American society, but here I will note just one pair: food and agriculture. Consider the calorie. If the train provides a visible example of culture, nature, and systems of control in the nineteenth century, the calorie provides an example of how a quantitative view of nature (in this case food) underlay production-oriented ways of treating nature in the twentieth century. The chemist W. O. Atwater identified the calorie as the central variable of food identity in the 1890s, offering a measurable unit through which to define food supplies. Earlier nutritional work had been spearheaded by vegetarians and utopians—Sylvester Graham, John Kellogg, and C. W. Post among them—who aimed to make aesthetics, moral concern, and spiritual connections to land and God the central criteria for good food. Early twentieth-century scientists instead followed the precepts of “scientific management,” the devotion to efficiency and streamlining that Frederick Taylor had pioneered on the factory floor.

As the historian nick Cullather has recently shown, the calorie also provided an important basis for u.S. foreign aid from the First World War until at least the 1970s. U.S. support of the Green Revolution, for example, represented this foreign policy approach.1313xSee nick Cullather, The Hungry World: Americaâ€™s Cold War Battle Against Poverty in Asia (Cambridge, MA: Harvard univerity Press, 2010); and Jessica Mudry, Measured Meals: Nutrition in America (Albany: State University of New York Press, 2009). A portable, measurable, disembodied number, the calorie followed from the same narrowing of vision that farmers applied to soil. Herbert Hoover, to take a salient case, would leverage that quantitative clarity to argue for food export programs in his role as Food Administrator during WWI. For him, calories represented units of energy suitable for a balance sheet and subject to maximization. not morality, justice, aesthetics, or taste but quantity would provide the basis for production goals in agricultural contexts as they emerged at the turn of the twentieth century.

I point to the calorie not just because it binds the quantified simplification of the control imperative to twentieth century governmental goals of promoting production, but also because it offers a reference to agriculture, one of the more visible, large-scale sites for public policy about the environment. What scholars have called the ascendant “productionist paradigm” of the twentieth century illustrates the trend toward maximization particularly well.1414xIn the interest of space, I am focusing on the American context, but the productionist paradigm was and remains intercontinental. See Tim Lang and Michael Heasman, Food Wars: The Global Battle for Mouths, Minds and Markets (London: Earthscan, 2004). In the productionist paradigm, agricultural policies should be devoted to maximizing production, yield and output are essential values, and the technologies of large-scale production provide an underlying mechanical basis.

Suffice it to say, maximizing production is, like control, efficiency, and optimization, a limitless gambit. There is no end to maximization, though it takes a good deal of effort to keep it going. Tractors, reapers, and harvesters operated mostly by organic (horses or oxen) and later mechanical (engines) power helped push a nineteenth-century bump in productivity. Between 1870 and 1900, with mechanical power and the vast expansion of the interior of the Great Plains, the united States saw the number of farms, acreage under the plow, and farm property value all double. The same era was witness to a doubling or more in the production of wheat, corn, cotton, cattle, and swine. Based on a fixation on productionism and the use of a USDA- supported regime of fertilizers, pesticides, herbicides, and other chemical management techniques, farmers helped promote the goals of increased production and productivity throughout the twentieth century. Policy-makers and farmers were committed to producing more, with ever quicker and more efficient strategies, assuming that productionism was a good in and of itself. Yet, after astonishing gains in yield and quantity in the latter decades of the nineteenth century and increased profit for American farmers in the first two decades of the twentieth—known as “the golden age of agriculture”—the depression, not surprisingly, cast doubt on the future of productionist logic.1515xdavid danbom, Born in the Country: A History of Rural America (Baltimore: Johns Hopkins University Press, 1995) 131 and 161.

Indeed, the depression introduced a kink in the unfettered commitment to production, as over-supply sat dormant at grain elevators, unable to be sold due to the drop in prices. The centerpiece of President Franklin D. Roosevelt’s first Agricultural Adjustment Act (1933) was production control, an effort by the government to pay farmers to keep products out of the market in an attempt to maintain market price stability. Yet, as one historian recently put it, even with this production control policy, surplus plagued the farm sector during the 1930s and would continue to do so throughout the twentieth century. For, as the USDA attempted to control production, it also researched and promoted technologies to modernize the farm and make the same acre of land produce more.1616xLaura Kolar, â€œFarmers in a Changing World,â€ 11. unpublished manuscript. Also see Kolar, â€œConserving the Country in Postwar America: Federal Conservation Policy from Eisenhower to nixon,â€ Ph.d. dissertation (Charlottesville: University of Virginia, 2011).

The government often worked at cross-purposes. The process of seeking more productivity was deliberate, the result of specific policies to shape fields, farmers, and emerging urban realities of the twentieth century. In many ways, that process defined underconsumption, not overproduction, as the key problem because production was a good in itself.

Ezra Taft Benson’s postwar USDA of the 1950s firmly entrenched that process and the productionist paradigm in Western agro-environmental politics. Local food and sustainable agriculture advocates today are generally familiar with the broad outlines of this paradigm, due in no small measure to its notice in Michael Pollan’s _Omnivore’s Dilemma_. Pollan draws on Earl Butz, nixon’s agriculture secretary, to represent the import of this view by the 1970s. Butz’s frequently repeated refrain that farms should plant “fencerow to fencerow” helps make the productionist goal easy to see. But the 1950s, not the 1970s, are more rightly the critical decade for the culmination of this view, the decade when Benson voiced the oft-quoted perspective that farmers should

“get big or get out.”

If present-day sustainability advocates decry the imperatives of a production-oriented farming structure by recalling post-War and nixon-era shifts, they might more emphatically walk it back to its ties to pre-War and turn-of-the-century infrastructural developments and the assumptions of a control-based view of nature developed over the prior century. Today’s argument for sustainability, that is, requires attention at the very least to a century’s worth of momentum toward unsustainable productionist practices. “The norms of productionism,” to quote the environmental ethicist Paul Thompson, are “deeply rooted.”1717xThompson 144.

## _The Consumerist Imperative_

“Consumptionism” is…the greatest idea that America has to give to the world; the idea that workmen and the masses be looked upon not simply as workers and producers, but as _consumers_.1818xChristine McGaffey Frederick, Selling Mrs. Consumer (New York: Business Bourse, 1929) 4â€“5.

If questions of unsustainable activity are bound up in the history of technology and industrialization and shaped by the growth of production-oriented policies, they are just as indebted to the consumer identity that developed in parallel with that productionist paradigm across the twentieth century. Consumption and production are as wedded as efficiency and productivity, the two walking hand in hand. And consumption, too, is limitless, in theory, especially within capitalist systems. Any ideas of sustainability that require people to observe the measure of nature, and not the measure of a marketplace, will challenge consumerism’s limitless draws on natural resources.

Though consumption and production come as a pair, the former’s historical salience followed from the latter’s earlier public success. At a general level, the nineteenth-century industrialists’ goal of increasing productive capacity fostered a culture of production.1919xSee Hounshell, From the American System to Mass Production; Merritt Roe Smith and Gregory Clancy, eds., Major Problems in the History of American Technology (New York: Houghton Mifflin, 1998). “When Americans before the twentieth century contemplated what made for the most robust national economy, the most stable American polity, and the most independent citizenry,” writes Lizabeth Cohen, “they overwhelmingly pointed to the vitality of production and the power of producers.”2020xLizabeth Cohen, A Consumerâ€™s Republic: The Politics of Mass Consumption in Postwar America (new York: Knopf, 2003) 21. To be sure, consumption existed as a cultural practice before the twentieth century, just as production was an industrial goal well after the nineteenth century. The shift I want to point to is a change in large-scale public priorities and a set of changes in how people defined themselves as moderns. By the first half of the twentieth century, that culture of production was secure enough to provide the foundation for other developments. In turn, the twentieth century fostered a culture of consumption.

Henry Ford informs that historical pivot from production to consumption. His view that “the necessary, precedent condition of mass production is a capacity, latent or developed, of mass consumption” provided the basis for the broader vision that, having mastered the principles of mass production, it was time to invent mass consumption.2121xFordâ€™s quotation is from his ghost-written entry to the 1926 Encyclopedia Britannica on â€œmass produc- tion.â€ He pushed the same point in his autobiography with the belief that â€œit is the function of business to produce for consumption.â€ See Henry Ford, My Life and Work (Garden City: Garden City, 1922) 12.

Thinkers from Antonio Gramsci forward have well characterized Fordism for its ideals of productivity, combining Smithian principles of the division of labor, manufacturing principles of the assembly line, and an overriding ethos that sought to control and manage resources from nature—rubber, oil, glass, iron, and steel. With those ideals, Ford offered another place-marker for the productionist paradigm sketched out above. At the same time, his plea to unlock latent consumption capacity set up a turn to the twentieth century’s consumerist paradigm. He certainly was not alone in this. When Christine Frederick, for example, a central figure in the household efficiency movement of the twentieth century, urged women to pursue feminist ideals through efficient strategies of domesticity, she was taking part in the same shift toward consumerist identity by helping create the professional modern consumer.2222xJanice Williams Rutherford, Selling Mrs. Consumer: Christine Frederick and the Rise of Household Efficiency (Athens: university of Georgia Press, 2003). See, also, Meg Jacobs, Pocketbook Politics: Economic Citizenship in Twentieth-Century America (Princeton: Princeton University Press, 2005) for more on the link between patriotism, civic duty, and consumption.

The consumer society arose from economic developments, to be sure, and yes, it drew conceptual support from the famous logic of industrial capitalism, but as with developments in productionism, it too was promoted as a matter of public policy, national identity, and civic duty. And just as industrialists built the principles of mass production in the nineteenth century with a culmination in the early twentieth, so did advocates promote the principles of mass consumption in the early twentieth century in ways that prepared their full import in the second half of the century. Wendell Berry summarizes the relationship clearly: “the specialization of production induces specialization of consumption.”2323xWendell Berry, The Pleasures of Eating, What Are People For? (San Francisco: north Point, 1990) 146.

Frederick, Ford, Hoover, and yet others offered observations on the early insinuation of that inducement. As Hoover put it, large-scale mass consumption was the highest stage of cultural development; public policymakers explicitly linked the very idea of progress and civilization to higher consumption rates.2424xHerbert Hoover, American Individualism (Garden City: Doubleday, Page, 1922) 33. The trend continued at the highest levels of government, even amidst the depression. Roosevelt argued in his 1932 presidential campaign that “we are at the threshold of a fundamental change in our popular economic thought, [such] that in the future we are going to think less about the producer and more about the consumer.”2525xFranklin d. Roosevelt, The Public Papers and Addresses of Franklin D. Roosevelt, Vol. 1, 1928“32 (new York: Random House, 1938) 639. I understand Hoover, Roosevelt, and the others’ goals as sincerely offered—that generating more consumption would encourage economic growth and improvements in the quality of life—even as I want to highlight the ways that this devotion perpetuated an endless goal, one that became even more entrenched during the Cold War.

Within the Cold War context of ideological bifurcation, the political economist Walt Rostow’s famous stages of growth may state the situation most clearly and emphatically. From the first stage of a “traditional society,” one of near subsistence and limited technological development, his stages eventually led through take-off and maturity to the pinnacle, evoking Hoover’s earlier view, of the “Age of High Mass Consumption.” Codified in writing in 1960, the Rostovian take-off model organized in one diagram a half-century of efforts to clarify the goal of consumption.2626xW. W. Rostow, The Stages of Economic Growth: A Non-Communist Manifesto (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1960). What Lizabeth Cohen has called a consumer’s republic, “an economy, culture, and politics built around the promises of mass consumption,” was given its foundation in the early part of the century but then fully formed in the early Cold War, “both in terms of material life and the more idealistic goals of freedom, democracy, and equality.”2727xCohen 7. It is no stretch to place President George Bush’s decree to the American public to “go shopping” after September 11th as an expression of the more current civic embodiment of this culture of consumption.

As the third layer within the historical lamination that also includes control and production, consumption might seem to offer a particular challenge to the biological and cultural parameters of limitlessness. After all, the cultural imperative for increased growth would meet a limit with biological constraints: there is only so much bodily consumption to go around. of consumer demand, John Kenneth Galbraith wondered about the same possible problem in 1958, asking, “if it continues on its geometric course will it not one day have to be constrained?”2828xJohn Kenneth Galbraith, How Much Should a Country Consume? Perspectives on Conservation: Essays on America's Natural Resources (Baltimore: Johns Hopkins University Press, 1958) 92. Yet little by little, through stiff-necked determination and a modern consumer drive fostered by production, marketing, policy, and no less than a moral argument for the heights of civilization, organic constraints on human consumption have not in practice finally defined a limit to consumerism.

From the roster of possible examples, sugar and sweeteners illustrate this broader trend well. Total caloric sweetener intake in the U.S. increased 39 percent in the second half of the twentieth century. Americans consumed eight times more corn sweetener across that same span. This pairs with increases in grain consumption of about a third, increases in fats and oils of about two-thirds, a fourfold increase in cheese consumption, and increases of about 40 percent in total meats (which includes a threefold increase in poultry and a modest 6 percent increase in red meat) over the same fifty-year period.2929xUSDA Agriculture Fact Book, 20012002 (Washington, DC: U.S. Government Printing office, 2002).

The statistics are arresting, especially given their outpacing of population growth. But, as the historian Shane Hamilton has written, “dry statistics mask the role of powerful government and business agents [in patterns of industrialization], as if the process were the product of inevitable technological forces or of the ‘logic’ of industrial capitalism.”3030xShane Hamilton, Trucking Country: The Road to Americas Wal-Mart Economy (Princeton: Princeton University Press, 2008) 101. The statistics, that is, are a surface register of contingent decisions that fostered consumption, decisions that we have forgotten and buried even as their consequences and assumptions about how humans live in nature—as demanding, extractive, and insatiable beings—carry forward.

As for those powerful agents working to create the consumerist imperative, the historian Carolyn de la Peña found artificial sweetener marketers inventing a concept called “prosperity stomach” in the mid-twentieth century, a guiding principle to encourage more consumption of low-cal drinks and a means to play out Rostow’s modernization theory in everyday life: more consumption equals more progress.3131xCarolyn de la PeÃ±a, Empty Pleasures: The Story of Artificial Sweeteners from Saccharin to Splenda (Chapel Hill: University of North Carolina Press, 2010). Like production, consumption was promoted as a good unto itself, not just with artificial sweeteners, but as a staple of late-modern public policy and full-blown personal consumer identity.

The rise of average daily caloric intake in the u.S. over the past few decades from 2,100 to 2,700 since 1980, along with those sugar and sweetener increases, is a testament to the success of increasing consumption in the face of arguments about limits and unhealthy or unsustainable practices.

Lest we think the trend for evermore consumption has abated in the face of health problems related to poor diets and over-consumption, de la Peña reports a recent discussion by diet soda consultants on a new concept called “stomach share.” Soda makers are fighting for our stomachs—not just defining the human body in quantified, _dis_ em-bodied terms, but moving on to internal organs. For them, as for Benson in the 1950s, the problem remains under-consumption. Increasing the share of a person’s stomach is the next frontier in taking the assumptions of the control, production, and consumption paradigms forward. In this pattern of limitless consumption, Atwater’s quantified calorie, Frederick’s professional consumer, the post-War’s consumer’s republic, and the control paradigm’s reduction of nature and food to numbers and calculations all come home to roost.3232x Carolyn de la Pe Stomach Share, And Everyday Life (28 october 2010): .

## Conclusion

It is an illusion to believe a sustainable future is one untouched by the past. Control, production, and consumption are also illusions, maintained by an historical sleight of hand that suggests the world is bounded only by the limits of our own ambitions. Control is always out of our grasp, encouraging us to keep pressing for it; production is an endless gambit, as is consumption. We moderns can make things and consume them, and we do that well, but toward what end is unclear and always just past the horizon.

Our twenty-first century culture has been good at keeping all of these illusions of sustainability going (control, productivism, consumerism). We do this by suggesting that unsustainability is a specific kind of problem that simply needs to be fixed, that ever increasing gains in efficiency will allow us to produce a greener future, that we can, with ample enough means, each individually buy our own environmental indulgences. This atomized, compartmentalized, and presentist approach to sustainability is a core feature of our current cultural moment, and this approach is the problem. It is a strategy that does not recognize the fundamentals of the unsustainable imperatives within which we operate, and which have emerged through a dedicated historical process. We need to acknowledge, redress, and reconfigure the momentum from these developments if we are to pursue any real, not illusory, effort at a sustainable future. The norms of unsustainability, that is, run deep.

One cultural consequence for those paradigmatically unsustainable developments is a buy-in to limitless resources and limitless ideals. Thus, even though environmental thinkers and advocates have, since the nineteenth century, brought into question the perception of limitless resources, the general character of our current society still pushes a set of cultural values that have no limits. It is not just that there are limits to growth, that is, but that we have produced an infrastructure of everyday life that depends on values that have no end. They are emergent and becoming, historical and temporal. Control, efficiency, productivity, and order—these are always in the process of reconstruction, becoming goals in and of themselves, and as such, they critically jeopardize our chances of building a society based upon a different value system. The values themselves—efficiency, productivity, etc.—are not necessarily good or bad on their own. Rather, it is the singular devotion to them as ends unto themselves that pres- ents difficulty for the goals of sustainable living. If our goal is one that has no end, then we can never stop aiming for it. As it were, aiming only for that kind of goal will keep us from ever achieving the nontechnical and nonquantitative goals we might otherwise define as sustainable metrics.

A related consequence is the continued reduction of the breadth of human values to narrow, disembodied terms while, in our environmental activities, we also treat nature in narrow ways. Just as one who seeks to dominate nature also seeks to dominate humans, so does one who seeks to reduce the breadth and complexity of nature seek to reduce the breadth and complexity of humanity. nourishing, communal, moral, aesthetic, and spiritual values fall to the side in the pursuit of control and order for the sake of production and consumption. The values that give foundation to modern relationships between human and non-human nature would be better placed as a means to the ends of moral and aesthetic dimensions, for example, rather than as the ends themselves.

The historical production of unsustainability was not pre-ordained. It was and remains an active result of public policy, cultural identity, and conceptual and physical development—of choices. Rather than a disembodied “logic” of industrial capitalism, we find a history of decisions made by various historical agents that built unsustainable structures and ways of living into our daily lives. The review of unsustainable development above suggests that without challenging the historically emerging imperatives of unsustainability, we have little chance to achieve a significant measure of improved environmental health, let alone a more just, nourishing, and equitable world. Without digging beneath the surface of things to the layer of relationships and intertwined conceptual foundations, we risk merely perpetuating the momentum of unsustainability that the past century and more has put in place.

We are as mushroom hunters in this way, believing that once we have taken the fruit from the mushroom, we have conquered it. But the great bulk of the mushroom is the mycelium beneath the surface, which grows, spreads, and intertwines to build a durable rhizomatic substrate out of our view. The mushrooms we pick are merely the fruit that sprouts from the surface, not the full life of the fungi itself. no matter what we do in the future, if we base our decisions only on the visible surface phenomena (the fruit) while avoiding the historically emergent depth of the infrastructure that gives substance to those visible features (the mycelium), we will remain indebted to the persistence of unsustainable patterns.
