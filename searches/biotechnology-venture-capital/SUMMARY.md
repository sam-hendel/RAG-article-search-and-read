## 1. Biotechnology and the Spirit of Capitalism

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** Spring 2006
**Word Count:** 6385
**Relevance Score:** 0.523

### Summary

The article examines how modern capitalism increasingly turns human life--especially the body--into a marketplace. It argues that this "commerce of the body" is expanding through biotechnology and related services that promise to improve, control, or even manipulate biological existence. Examples include reproductive technologies such as egg donation, embryo use for stem-cell research, and the possibility of producing or trading embryos as medical commodities; medical interventions such as anti-impotence drugs and anti-depressants marketed as solutions to personal suffering; and consumer-style bodily modification such as cosmetic surgery and genetic selection in IVF. The piece places these developments in a historical and moral context by describing three "spirits" of early capitalism: religious "calling" and salvation through work, Voltaire's irreverent self-love and comfort in worldly exchange, and Adam Smith's moderation and belief that commerce can improve society while staying within moral limits. It then contends that biotechnology and biocapitalism heighten a problem already present in capitalism: the desire for salvation--whether heavenly or earthly--being redirected into material progress. Overall, it questions whether bio-capitalism represents a fundamental moral crisis, warning that treating sacred human goods as purchasable commodities may erode human dignity, responsibility, and the distinction between what should be pursued and what should not.

## 2. In Me We Trust

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Spring 2014
**Issue:** Europe in Search of Europeans
**Section:** Essays
**Word Count:** 4454
**Relevance Score:** 0.404

### Summary

The article argues that biotechnology is undergoing an "enclosure" of shared resources, especially the genome, and that this trend functions less like a traditional "tragedy of the commons" than as a "tragedy of the anti-commons," where monopolies block nonowners from using and benefiting from resources. It first connects this idea to skepticism about public blood donation in Europe and then focuses on genetic privatization: genes and gene-based diagnostic access are increasingly controlled through patents held largely by private firms. The discussion centers on patenting disputes highlighted by the Angelina Jolie case and the U.S. Supreme Court's ruling in the Myriad decision, contrasting earlier failures to challenge patents in other contexts, such as the Greenberg case. The article then broadens from genetics to public health, examining how distrust of public institutions undermines vaccination ("We Medicine") and how herd immunity resembles a commons. It links growing vaccine skepticism to political and economic forces associated with neoliberalism, including policies that subsidize private biotech while privatizing biomedical benefits. Finally, it proposes alternatives to enclosure: models for commons governance, charitable-trust biobanks, patient-researcher shared patenting, and initiatives like "Free the Data" and Cancer Commons that aim to keep genetic information accessible and governed for public health and research.

## 3. Biotechnology by the Numbers

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** Spring 2003
**Word Count:** 475
**Relevance Score:** 0.377

### Summary

The article describes a U.S. Department of Commerce survey aimed at producing the government's first detailed overview of the biotechnology industry's size and current condition. Titled "Critical Technology Assessment of Biotechnology in U.S. Industry," the effort will collect information about the new science pipeline, public and private investment levels in biotechnology research and development, and the economic health and competitiveness of U.S. biotech firms. It also focuses on issues such as barriers to competition and the extent of international collaborations involving foreign partners, reflecting biotechnology's importance for both healthcare and national security. Because biotechnology has not previously been treated as a distinct industry in U.S. data systems, the survey also confronts an unresolved definitional problem: what qualifies as "biotechnology." The article contrasts a Commerce Department meeting's reported disagreement with BIO's working definition, which centers on using biological processes to create useful products or solve problems. The survey targets roughly 3,000 firms and asks whether they operate in "human health" by using biological entities (cells, genes, proteins, enzymes, antibodies, etc.) to address disease and genetic disorders. It further asks companies to identify specific biotechnology activities, spanning DNA-based work (genomics, bioinformatics), biochemistry areas (vaccines and drug design), bioprocessing (manipulating cells/tissues/embryos), and environmental biotechnology. Results will be summarized in a report due August 2003.

## 4. Biocapitalism

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** Summer 2006
**Word Count:** 1146
**Relevance Score:** 0.367

### Summary

The article centers on how biotechnology and modern commerce shape human life, especially as markets increasingly commercialize the body. Eric Cohen's discussion begins with the idea that technology and commerce must be reconsidered in relation to one another, arguing that capitalism's momentum can overwhelm cultural institutions meant to restrain its moral excesses. The text claims that markets effectively package harms behind undeniable benefits, enabling expansion into sectors like health care and higher education and ultimately into areas of bodily control--such as cosmetic surgery, neurochemistry, assisted reproduction, and potential future "organ vendors." Two responses to Cohen expand the debate. Daniel Callahan argues that conservative concern should focus less on thinkers and more on markets dominating the moral foundations that previously balanced capitalism's harms and benefits. Nick Schulz introduces the concept of "techno-physio evolution," emphasizing how knowledge in medicine and biotechnology changes human physical and mental conditions, raising the question of whether such changes might make certain "permanent questions" about being human no longer permanent. Cohen's reply accepts that technology alters human life's contours but largely maintains that core problems--suffering, death, and moral meaning--persist. He also highlights new challenges created by modern biotech, including questions about natality and the reasons for having children in an era of birth control and advanced reproduction technologies. He concludes that dialogue and moral guidance are needed so business activity serves citizens broadly, not merely shareholders.

## 5. Why Conservatives Care About Biotechnology

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** Summer 2003
**Word Count:** 4472
**Relevance Score:** 0.240

### Summary

The article examines why conservatives in the United States have growing reservations about biotechnology, especially reproductive and genetic technologies. It argues that the advance of biotech is not a harmless, value-neutral set of tools, but a force that raises major moral, social, and philosophical threats. First, conservatives worry that some biotech developments require violating "basic moral commandments," particularly around the moral status of embryos. Human cloning and cloning-for-research are presented as cases where embryo destruction and experimentation become normalized, effectively treating human beings as experimental material. The article connects these concerns to longer-running life-issues debates while emphasizing that conservatives often see research and "industrial" embryo experimentation as more alarming than other possibilities. Second, it claims biotech will reshape how society thinks about parenthood, family, autonomy, and equality. The article highlights a conservative concern that treating children as products would change public attitudes, even before particular practices become widespread. It also argues that genetic selection and engineering introduce a new kind of power over future persons, challenging liberal ideals. Third, the article discusses the fear that biotechnology can function as an ideology--distorting views of human nature through both utopian promises and real-world effects. Finally, it places the debate within America's liberal political project, suggesting conservatives should regulate biotech, enforce "bright lines," and engage public opinion to protect core liberal principles.
