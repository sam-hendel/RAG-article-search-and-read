## 1. Capital Inequalities

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Summer 2021
**Issue:** Distinctions That Define and Divide
**Section:** Thematic Essays
**Word Count:** 5297

### Summary

The article examines how inequality is reproduced through "noneconomic" resources such as cultural knowledge, social connections, and symbolic recognition--especially among elites and their institutions. It argues that common understandings of these resources are distorted by two conceptual burdens: "elite determinism" (the assumption that elites fully determine culture and that cultural advantage simply trickles down) and "economic determinism" (the tendency to treat culture and social life as if they operated like money, where value is fungible and independent of the person holding it). To clarify these issues, the essay traces intellectual roots in Thorstein Veblen and Pierre Bourdieu. Veblen's account explains elite power through symbolic status consumption and imitation dynamics, but the essay challenges his tendency to overstate elite dominance and to ignore how culture can be created through cross-class appropriation and conflict. Bourdieu's framework replaces trickle-down with "distinction," where groups struggle to mark boundaries through taste and practice. However, the essay contends that Bourdieu's influential "capital" metaphor still smuggles in an economic analogy that can mislead people about how value actually works. A central claim is that returns on cultural or social resources are contingent on ascriptive identity (race, gender, sexuality, etc.). Thus, opening institutions does not automatically produce equality: marginalized people may acquire similar competencies, but recognition and valuation differ, sustaining durable inequalities even in more "meritocratic" settings.

## 2. Social Science or Social Activism?

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Spring 2007
**Issue:** Intellectuals and Public Responsibility
**Section:** Book Reviews
**Word Count:** 1271

### Summary

The article is a review of Charles F. Gattone's book *The Social Scientist as Public Intellectual*. It centers on a longstanding question in the social sciences: whether scholars should remain detached from public life to preserve balanced scholarship, or whether the nature of social science makes such involvement unavoidable. It frames the core issue as a fragile relationship between knowledge and ideology, and between scholarly analysis and political action--particularly the tension between understanding social structures and judging whether they should be changed. The review describes Gattone's approach: he treats social scientific knowledge as interpretive and responsibility-bearing, not merely informational, and examines what is at stake in how social scientists should engage publicly. To do this, Gattone discusses nine major thinkers--Comte, Saint-Simon, Weber, Veblen, Mannheim, Schumpeter, Mills, Galbraith, and Bourdieu--tracing how their historical contexts shaped their views on whether and how social scientists should act as public intellectuals. Key themes include rationalization in modern society, the academy's constraints on intellectual originality, the ethical and prescriptive demands of public engagement, and the political pressures arising from events like the rise of fascism. The review also highlights a concern that the book does not develop a deeper analytic synthesis, especially regarding distinctions between public intellectuals and activists.

## 3. Commodification and Consumer Society

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Summer 2003
**Issue:** The Commodification of Everything
**Section:** Bibliographic Review
**Word Count:** 3792

### Summary

The article is a bibliographic review of scholarly work on **commodification** and **consumer society**. It frames commodification as a longstanding concern, but one that has intensified alongside modern consumerism and major political-economic changes, especially the shift toward free markets in former communist states and the expansion of market logic into new social domains. It explains that commodification scholarship is broad, with competing assessments: some see commercialization as enabling freedom, wealth, creativity, and efficiency, while others warn about social inequality, exploitation, corruption of values, and environmentally damaging consumption patterns. The review outlines the literature in stages. It first surveys **classic theorists**, largely tied to Marxist and related traditions that emphasize class relations, production, alienation, and the "fetishization" of commodities, as well as status and prestige in consumption (Weber, Veblen). It then covers **contemporary approaches** that shift attention toward consumption itself--especially the symbolic meaning of goods--along with theories linked to postmodernism (notably Baudrillard's simulacra). Next, it traces **historical origins of consumer culture**, including earlier precedents in preindustrial and early modern periods, and the role of U.S. **postwar affluence**. It also reviews research on **advertising and shopping**, then addresses **ethical frameworks** for evaluating commodification, and concludes with both **celebrations of free markets** and **recent critiques** involving branding, overspending, emotional labor, and new luxury-driven consumption.

## 4. Higher Education in Americaby Derek Bok

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Summer 2015
**Issue:** The Body in Question
**Section:** Book Reviews
**Word Count:** 902

### Summary

The article examines the modern American university as a "multiversity"--a large, complex institution that combines many functions beyond teaching. Using the University of Virginia as an example, it describes a university operating medical services, running research centers, hosting major entertainment events, supporting business incubation, coordinating sustainability efforts, serving community and government needs, sponsoring athletics, and conducting extensive research alongside educating tens of thousands of students. The discussion traces this model to Clark Kerr's concept from the 1960s, when postwar enrollment growth and federal funding expanded universities into managed conglomerates governed by administrators and money rather than a single coherent purpose. Derek Bok's book, *Higher Education in America*, is presented as defending this multiversity model and arguing that its multiple missions--teaching, research, economic development, and public service--create productive "synergies" that make American universities stronger and more capable. However, the article also notes Bok's concession that the pursuit of many competing goals produces confusion, bureaucratic growth, and institutional competition for resources and prestige. It argues that the "basic academic values" Bok invokes (such as research honesty, scholarly impartiality, freedom of thought, and teaching quality) lack firm grounding within the multiversity, suggesting that these ideals belong more naturally to the traditional research university or broader academy, which the article implies may be weakening.

## 5. Invisible Science

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Fall 2016
**Issue:** The Cultural Contradictions of Modern Science
**Section:** The Cultural Contradictions Of Modern Science
**Word Count:** 5196

### Summary

The article examines how science permeates everyday life even when no laboratory work is visible. Using a nearby McDonald's in Cambridge as a starting point, it argues that fast food functions as an "embedded" scientific site: the ingredients, food safety systems, nutritional labeling, refrigeration and building infrastructure, and many production and marketing decisions rely on extensive scientific and technical expertise. This includes outsourced scientific work (e.g., inspections, ingredient sourcing standards, packaging research) and internal corporate "food laboratories" and analytics that test flavors, stability, consumer responses, and restaurant design. The article then connects this embedded science to broader social patterns. It discusses public debates about obesity, diabetes, and the role of expertise in regulating sugary foods, illustrating how scientific authority is contested. It also argues that the state itself "floats on a sea of science" through statistics, risk calculations, surveillance, and various forms of measurement that make governance possible. A major claim is that embedded science--especially in the "human sciences" (psychology, sociology, anthropology, economics, linguistics)--shapes how people understand themselves and others, making "soft" disciplines as culturally consequential as "hard" ones. Finally, it addresses the "scientization of the ordinary," suggesting that multiple, competing sciences coexist and can be invoked to challenge particular forms of embedded expertise.

## 6. The Endless Pursuit of Better

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Summer 2021
**Issue:** Distinctions That Define and Divide
**Section:** Thematic Essays
**Word Count:** 4124

### Summary

The article argues that the "meritocratic" American elite has evolved into an "aspirational class" that pursues constant self-improvement while treating its preferences as inherently moral, culturally superior, and widely shared. It contends that many of the aspirational class's defining behaviors--elite education pipelines, distinctive consumer choices, politically progressive stances, and virtue-signaling lifestyles--depend on inherited wealth and cultural capital that are not realistically accessible to most Americans. Because these advantages are often invisible to those who possess them, aspirational elites come to believe their success reflects pure effort, reinforcing a "meritocracy myth" while leaving structural inequality largely intact. To explain how status reproduces itself, the article draws on Pierre Bourdieu's concept of cultural capital and habitus: education, speech patterns, tastes, and networks function as social markers that signal belonging. It rejects simplistic claims that the main divide is rural versus coastal, suggesting instead that Americans differ in the language, motivations, and cultural signifiers they use even when economic and educational attainment overlap. Finally, it places these dynamics within American political polarization. Rather than purely class or regional economics, the article emphasizes mismatched cultural capital and mutual condescension, which can intensify anti-intellectualism and resentment. It concludes that "left behind" voters may not be wholly unlike elites, but are often responding to exclusion, snobbery, and limited pathways into the aspirational game.

## 7. Virtuosos of Idleness

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Spring 2018
**Issue:** The Human and the Digital
**Section:** Essays
**Word Count:** 5408

### Summary

The article examines the Bloomsbury Group--associated with Virginia Woolf, John Maynard Keynes, Lytton Strachey, and Bertrand Russell--as a key early twentieth-century set of thinkers and writers who rethought "work," "idleness," and the cultural meaning of leisure. It begins by contrasting the group's reputation for snobbish laziness with evidence that Bloomsbury produced enduring novels, philosophical essays, and economic ideas that directly engage how people should live when relieved from necessity. The discussion then shifts to contemporary anxieties about work and leisure: automation, coercive employment conditions, precariousness, and the ways "free time" can become restless distraction or mere recovery for the next day's labor. Against this backdrop, the article interprets Russell and Keynes as forecasting reduced working hours and a society where leisure could support civilization--though they also warn leisure may produce misery without moral and cultural change. Strachey is used to complicate the work ethic by depicting Victorian industry as either pathological (Nightingale, obsessed and bodily wrecked) or monotonous-duty bound (Queen Victoria), implying that work can be misguided. Finally, Woolf's treatment of idleness is central: she distinguishes sloth from valuable forms of activity and elevates overlooked labor (including women's domestic and caring work), while also presenting a vivid figure of vacant lethargy to test what "leisure" really can mean.

## 8. Whatever Happened to General Education?

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Spring 2017
**Issue:** The Post-Modern Self
**Section:** Essays
**Word Count:** 6384

### Summary

The article examines why "general education" in American universities has failed to produce shared intellectual or moral life among students and asks what pluralism and democracy can still look like on campus. It begins with Thorstein Veblen's critique of early research universities as "competitive businesses" driven by money and social status rather than learning, then contrasts that diagnosis with the lived experience of students at the University of Virginia: classroom study seems disconnected from campus life shaped by fraternities, athletics, gendered treatment, and status-driven extracurricular culture. From this, the author argues that universities have adopted "check-box" distribution curricula that fragment knowledge into departments and treat choice as the only educational virtue. This institutional structure reflects broader cultural and political trends, including neoliberal market values that reduce education to personal economic return, while leaving curricula lacking a robust conception of the goods education can serve. The article then traces the historical origins of general education debates from late nineteenth-century specialization to twentieth-century reform efforts at institutions like Columbia and the University of Chicago. It recounts a key conflict over how to unify knowledge: Hutchins and Adler's "great books" program grounded in fixed metaphysical principles versus the New Plan's survey approach and later Chicago Core. The author concludes by proposing a curriculum as an assertion of shared values and purposes without requiring a single comprehensive moral consensus, framing the university as a potential "bulwark" for democratic, pluralist discourse.

## 9. How the State Built This AI Moment

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** Summer 2023
**Word Count:** 3078

### Summary

The article argues that today's "AI moment" and accompanying debates about existential risk are shaped not by runaway technology outpacing law, but by deliberate government involvement in creating and promoting advanced AI. It critiques the common "law-lag narrative," which portrays policymakers as perpetually behind technological change--reacting too slowly to risks posed by powerful systems. Instead, the piece claims that governments have actively "bred" AI through strategies, funding, and partnerships intended to move AI from research to commercialization and deployment across sectors like healthcare, defense, and agriculture. The article surveys public alarm and advocacy for urgent governance--ranging from calls for AI pauses to warnings about uncontrollable "god-like" AI--then contrasts these with evidence of state investment and institutional support. It describes the proliferation of AI ethical and policy initiatives, such as national AI principles and international guidelines via organizations like the OECD, emphasizing that many of these function as "soft law" mechanisms and self-assessment tools rather than enforceable rules. Finally, it examines the EU's proposed AI Act and its risk-based framework, including how general-purpose AI was initially excluded and obligations were oriented toward users and deployers. Overall, the piece calls for shifting attention from catching up to asking democratic questions about what futures AI should enable, who decides, and how policy should shape or limit AI's broader desirability and deployment.

## 10. Reviving Expertise in a Populist Age

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** Fall 2019
**Word Count:** 5081

### Summary

The article examines how American politics has become polarized over the role of expertise and how this conflict has contributed to distrust in scientific authority and democratic institutions. It contrasts arguments that blame "ignorance" and populist politics for democratic decline with proposals for transferring more power to the knowledgeable or educated--such as "epistocracy"--and with fears that technocracy means corrupt, unaccountable rule by an elite "expert" class. It surveys why technocracy is criticized: it can undermine democratic equality, increase risks of abuse of state power (including historical examples of scientific eugenics and authoritarian planning), and fail in practice due to the limits of capturing complex social realities with abstract models. It also notes that experts are not immune to bias, self-interest, or the distortion that comes with political power. The article then focuses on the U.S. separation of powers, arguing that Congress has increasingly delegated lawmaking authority to administrative agencies, shifting major policy choices--especially technical ones--away from elected representatives and toward executive-branch expertise with limited accountability. In response to Congress's institutional weakness and lack of scientific capacity, it highlights reforms such as reviving the Office of Technology Assessment (OTA). The OTA is presented as a model where expert assessments informed democratic decision-making without making policy or exercising legislative authority, helping Congress scrutinize executive and industry claims. The article concludes that strengthening congressional access to transparent, value-aware technical analysis can counter technocratic drift while preserving democratic control.

## 11. The Case Against “STEM”

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** Winter 2021
**Word Count:** 8769

### Summary

The article argues against treating "STEM" as a single blended category and against the tendency to blur the boundary between science and technology in public policy. It begins by challenging a widespread "Baconian" assumption: that science exists mainly as a tool for technological and economic progress. The author traces this view to Francis Bacon, who promoted judging science by its "fruits" and emphasized experimentation and the utility of knowledge for "power" over nature--yet the article insists that this rhetoric has been misunderstood and has led to policy errors. Although science and technology have become interdependent, the piece argues they remain distinct enterprises with different aims, professional identities, and standards of success. It reviews history to dispute simplified versions of the relationship: industrial revolutions relied heavily on skilled artisans and mechanical know-how rather than on cutting-edge scientific theory, while early scientific advances were not uniformly driven by technology. The nineteenth century increased overlap, but the institutions did not merge; science professionalized into "pure" knowledge, while technology developed its own education and engineering culture. Finally, the author warns that conflation endangers both fields, especially by crowding out "pure" research when funding prioritizes immediate applications. The article concludes that preserving science's capacity requires defending it as valuable in itself, not only for technological outcomes.

## 12. The Invention of the War Machine

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** Spring 2014
**Word Count:** 7110

### Summary

The article argues that the First World War did not "invent" most of the famous technologies associated with it; instead, many wartime weapon systems and practices were modifications, scaling, and militarized versions of technologies already developed for civilian or earlier uses. It surveys wartime technology in three areas: weapons, medical innovations, and non-weapons technologies. In weapons, the submarine, hydrophone detection, depth charges, machine-gun interrupter gear for aircraft, and artillery-related advances are presented as examples of adaptation rather than wholly new invention. Even major "war" innovations are treated as limited or derivative--such as tanks drawing on prewar engines and steel, and chemical weapons relying on earlier industrial chemistry rather than creating new chemical engineering. In medicine, the war is shown as a catalyst for wider adoption of existing tools and methods (mobile X-rays, bandages, motorized evacuation, blood transfusion practices, blood preservation, early blood banking). It also contributed to advances tied to trauma, including recognition and later treatment development for shell shock, growth of modern plastic surgery, and the war's role in reinforcing experimental medical approaches. The article's central theme is institutional: the greatest "invention" of World War I was the war machine--an integrated system linking industry, academic science, and government. It focuses on two Americans, Arthur D. Little (industrial research and corporate lab models) and George Ellery Hale (professionalizing science and creating the National Research Council), showing how wartime cooperation helped form early versions of the military-industrial complex.

## 13. The Painless Peace ofTwilight Sleep

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** Fall 2007
**Word Count:** 3163

### Summary

The article examines Edith Wharton's novel **_Twilight Sleep_** as a sustained critique of the modern, post-World War I American culture that believes science and commerce can eliminate suffering, solve moral problems, and remake human life. It contrasts _Twilight Sleep_ with Wharton's better-known early works and discusses its poor initial reception and later critical judgments, then focuses on what the novel depicts: a socially prominent New York household led by **Pauline Manford**, a relentless "do-gooder" and disciple of modern remedies. At the center is the "**twilight sleep**" medical practice--mixes of scopolamine and morphine used to dull the pain of childbirth. In the novel it becomes symbolic of a larger worldview: a drive to avoid physical pain, manage morality through health categories, and treat babies like products. The article also traces how Pauline's faith in psychoanalysis, self-help, hygiene, spiritual "operations," and eugenics turns genuine feeling into avoidance, producing emotional emptiness and social shallowness. The plot's domestic melodrama--affair, scandal, attempted violence--serves as the vehicle for broader themes: modern optimism and progress obscure the past and deny human nature, while efforts to erase pain lead to alienation from others and catastrophic consequences. It argues that **Nona's** turn toward empathy and moral responsibility rejects the fantasy of a pain-free, perfected humanity.
