## 1. Interview with James E. Young

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Summer 2007
**Issue:** The Uses of the Past
**Section:** Interview
**Word Count:** 1765
**Relevance Score:** 0.925

### Summary

The article is an interview with James E. Young about how societies remember mass violence and the role of memorials in shaping memory. Young questions assumptions that memorializing automatically preserves truthful or meaningful remembrance. He argues that Holocaust memorial impulses can be driven by a desire to forget, especially when memorials fix the past into a single, authoritative site that may "cap" further memory-work for future generations. Young introduces "memory-work" to emphasize remembrance as an ongoing process of negotiation and changing relationships to the past, rather than a static product. He also critiques loose psychoanalytic language applied to nations, preferring to distinguish "collected memory" from "collective memory": memorial sites may gather diverse experiences into a shared space while assigning singular meanings that support unity for institutions such as nation-states or religious groups. He differentiates memorials from monuments, and explores "counter-monuments"--works that resist traditional monumental certainty through provocation, change, impermanence, absence, and demands for visitor interaction. Young also warns that memorials can become idols that close off memory, substitute for political action, or even function to help people forget. Drawing comparisons across projects in Germany, Argentina, and the United States, he highlights both cultural differences and common tensions about mourning, atonement, and national narratives. He further addresses how group identities can develop through a "culture of competing catastrophes," where remembered suffering becomes political leverage.

## 2. A Conversation with Artist Karen Parker Lears on “Swansquarter: The Wound-Dresser’s Dreaming”

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Summer 2010
**Issue:** The Phantom Economy
**Section:** Interview
**Word Count:** 1546
**Relevance Score:** 0.913

### Summary

The article is a conversation with artist Karen Parker Lears about her series of works, "Swansquarter: The Wound-Dresser's Dreaming." It connects her varied life and careers--biological study, medical internship at Valley Forge Army Hospital during the Vietnam War, and later work as an editor--to her artistic practice, which blends close observation, deep materiality, and memory with abstraction. Lears describes the series as growing from years of making canvases as "memory palaces" and "memory theater," shaped by personal experiences of loss and witnessing suffering in her family. "Swansquarter" centers on the question of what people do "in the presence of wounds," including wounds to soldiers and civilians and the overlooked costs to flesh, mind, spirit, families, and community. She frames the works from the perspective of the wound tender, treating tending, tenderness, dreaming, and refuge as routes through loss, and suggesting that wounds can function as portals or maps. The article highlights recurring resonant materials--broken barbed wire, smashed automobile safety glass, and confetti--along with stories behind them, such as confetti given by her young daughter during her father's dying. Lears says the series is not didactic politics, but aims to unsettle conscience and make society more aware of wounded people, pressing viewers to confront their role as bystanders.

## 3. History and Memory

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Summer 2007
**Issue:** The Uses of the Past
**Section:** Book Reviews
**Word Count:** 1196
**Relevance Score:** 0.908

### Summary

The article is a review of Jay Winter's book *Remembering War*, which examines why the twentieth century became so preoccupied with memory. It argues that the "memory boom" seen in museums, public history, and the arts has roots in World War I, and that later developments--especially reactions to the Second World War--expanded and reshaped it. Winter traces this shift by focusing on shell shock as a pivotal cultural and medical category. During World War I, attempts to represent the disorder through medicine, psychology, and literature helped transform ideas about how violence affects the self, linking trauma-like experiences to identity. After the war, commemoration took many forms, including photographs, war memorials, and collections of letters. The review then summarizes Winter's discussion of a later "second generation" of memory beginning in the 1970s and 1980s, driven by accountability for Nazi crimes, the rise of identity politics, growing affluence, scientific validation of trauma, and historians' attention to how people construct meaning. The concluding sections emphasize the overlap between history and memory and warn against treating all accounts as the same thing. The review also raises a question about whether "acknowledging victims" is always a universal moral or psychological necessity, noting that Holocaust remembrance did not immediately surge after 1945 and that notions like trauma vary across societies.

## 4. Bibliographic Review on the Uses of the Past

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Summer 2007
**Issue:** The Uses of the Past
**Section:** Bibliographic Review
**Word Count:** 2723
**Relevance Score:** 0.851

### Summary

The article is a bibliographic review of scholarship on how the past is used, remembered, narrated, and contested in the present. It argues that modern people and societies repeatedly draw on earlier events and stories--whether through entertainment, political calls for tradition, national myths, or violent ideological justifications. It frames this preoccupation with memory as intertwined with rapid social change and with the recognition that history and memory can be shaped, manipulated, or weaponized. A major focus is "collective memory," including foundational theory from Maurice Halbwachs about how social groups construct memories through shared frameworks, and how collective memory differs from historical memory. The review also organizes research on national identity, emphasizing how nations use rituals, monuments, commemorations, and invented traditions to make their origins seem ancient and meaningful. Other sections examine how individuals narrate themselves using culturally available pasts; how mass suffering and atrocity create challenges for documentation, especially when eyewitness testimony, partial recollection, and perpetrator efforts to destroy evidence shape available records; and how commemoration can enable forgetting or "whitewashing," including nostalgia that prettifies injustice. Finally, it surveys work linking historical interpretation to morality and education, such as debates over religious authority, reparations, museum and school curricula, and ethics in remembering.

## 5. From Usable Pasts to the Return of the Repressed

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Summer 2007
**Issue:** The Uses of the Past
**Section:** Essays
**Word Count:** 5557
**Relevance Score:** 0.841

### Summary

The article examines how scholars relate past and present in theories of memory, identity, and historical power. It begins with three major approaches. The "usable past" view treats historical referents as inventions or reconstructions shaped by present needs. A closely related line in social science--presentist and instrumental--argues that collective memory is remade in the present for present goals, enabling "politics of memory" to expose how interests manipulate the past (as in Halbwachs and Hobsbawm). Critics find these views inadequate because collective memory often resists such remaking, and because they do not explain why the past is effective as an instrument. The article then turns to trauma theory, particularly psychoanalytic ideas of repression, latency, and "return of the repressed," which frame how past suffering grips later individuals and societies. It critiques the widespread use of "trauma" to interpret collective and even distant suffering, raising ontological problems about applying individual-psychological concepts to groups ("category mistake" concerns inspired by Durkheim). To address this, the author revisits Freud's controversial attempt to generalize traumatic memory to the history of Jewish monotheism in *Moses and Monotheism*. The essay surveys defenses and critiques of Freud (Yerushalmi, Derrida, Bernstein, Assmann), then proposes additional frameworks--Bourdieu's habitus and Bakhtin's genre theory--to explain how repressed or latent cultural histories can persist, transmit across generations, and reappear without reducing social memory to a literal "group psyche."
