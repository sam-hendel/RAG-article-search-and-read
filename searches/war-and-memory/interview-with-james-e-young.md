# Interview with James E. Young

*“Memorials seem to remember just about everything except their own coming into being.”*

**_You have written that “the initial impulse to memorialize events like the Holocaust may actually spring from an opposite and equal desire to forget them.”_ _1_ 1xJames E. Young, _The Texture of Memory: Holocaust Memorials and Meaning_ (New Haven: Yale University Press, 1994) 5. _What does that suggest for how we should remember these events?_**

When I wrote this, I was thinking specifically of the deep skepticism a post–World War II generation of artists and architects shared about Holocaust memorialization in Germany. During the heated and fascinating debates over what has become the national _Memorial to Europe’s Murdered Jews_ (dedicated in 2005 in Berlin), many young German artists in particular feared that by concretizing and fixing Holocaust memory in a single site in Berlin, such a memorial might somehow draw a bottom-line beneath the Holocaust, serving as a kind of capstone that would in effect bury the Holocaust and thereby foreclose further Holocaust memory-work by future generations.

_**What do you mean by “memory-work”?**_

I use the term “memory-work” to emphasize the process of memory over a single, static result. “Memory-work” also connotes a kind of “working-through,” which includes a sense of our changing relationship to particular memories. Memory of the past might thus be regarded as a constantly negotiated, animated dialogue between ourselves and the past. I also hope this term suggests that the search for memory is also always a search for meaning in such memory.

_**Often psychological terms used to describe individuals are applied to collective entities like states or societies, such as the suggestion that a society has repressed a certain memory. What do you think of this practice—of the reasons for it and the possible problems associated with it?**_

While I share many of the assumptions and premises of psychoanalytic thought on memory in individuals, I do balk at applying these terms too loosely to collective entities, like nations and religious groups. I know some of this gets back to that early divide between personal memory and the social constructions of memory and its meanings, but I do believe that individuals cannot share another’s memory of particular events any more than we can share each other’s cortex. However, we do often share common spaces of memory and the common meanings they assign to otherwise highly individual memories of the same events. But do entire societies “repress” memories too painful to bear, as either victim or perpetrator? I don’t think so.

_**You make the distinction between “collective memory” and “collected memory.” What is the difference between the two and why do you prefer the latter?**_

In the strictly social-constructivist sense in which Maurice Halbwachs first referred to “collective memory,” I think the term is still quite apt. But in recent years, there has been a tendency to use the term a little too loosely, asking it to refer, for example, to a nation’s seemingly shared memory. Here specifically in the context of memorial sites, I wanted to distinguish between the illusion of “shared memory” these sites would propagate and the actuality of disparate, even competing “collected memories” gathered in such sites. That is, I believe that memorials, rather than “collectivizing” actual memory, assign singular, even collective meaning to the multiplicity of memories of all the different visitors to such sites. “Collected memory” thus allows for many, possibly conflicting memories to be gathered in common spaces, where they are then assigned singular meaning—by the nation-state, religious community, or any other social group with an interest in creating unity among its disparate parts and individuals.

_**What is the difference between a memorial and a monument?**_

In _The Texture of Memory_ , I suggested that we distinguish memorials from monuments in their broad, generic senses. I wrote:

> There are memorial books, memorial activities, memorial days, memorial festivals, and memorial sculptures. Some of these are mournful, some celebratory: but all are memorials in a larger sense. Monuments, on the other hand, will refer here to a subset of memorials: the material objects, sculptures, and installations used to memorialize a person or thing. For the purposes of this book, I treat all memory-sites as memorials, the plastic objects within these sites as monuments. A memorial may be a day, a conference, or a space, but it need not be a monument. A monument, on the other hand, is always a kind of memorial.22xYoung 4.

_**What is a counter-monument?**_

In its earliest versions, “counter-monument” referred literally to a monument built adjacent to an existing monument as a kind of negation of it, as a way to counter its meaning and symbolic significance. But later, I began to think of counter-monuments also as those monuments that challenge the traditional premises of the monument. I think Maya Lin’s _Vietnam Veterans Memorial_ may be this country’s most prominent counter-monument for the ways it counter-points the capitol’s more conventional neoclassic, triumphalist memorial statuary. Cut into the earth, inviting visitors to come face-to-face with the names of soldiers killed in the Vietnam War, it seemed to articulate formally America’s great ambivalence toward the memory of the war and its veterans.

Most of my thought on counter-monuments, however, focuses on the provocative memorial designs in Germany by a post–World War II generation of artists and architects who were deeply skeptical of what they regarded as the traditional monument’s fascist and authoritarian pedigree. In this generation’s eyes, the didactic logic of monuments—their demagogical rigidity and certainty of history, their tendency to reduce the individual to insignificance—recalled too closely traits they associated with Nazism itself. For many of these artists, the notion of commemorating the victims of fascism in such forms had grown untenable. Counter-monuments by Jochen Gerz, Esther Shalev, and Horst Hoheisel, among others, thus flouted any number of traditional memorial conventions: their aim was not to console but to provoke; not to remain fixed but to change; not to be everlasting but to disappear; not to be ignored by its passersby but to demand interaction. The void created by the mass murder of Europe’s Jews would now be commemorated with further voids and absences—hence, their disappearing, invisible, and negative-form memorial spaces.

_**Most of us think of memorials as designed to help us remember something and in an appropriate way. And yet, you have remarked that sometimes we relegate our work of remembering to these memorials, such that we free ourselves from that responsibility.**_  _**You have even argued that sometimes monuments help us to forget. Could you say more about this?**_

To the extent that we see memorials as finished objects in the landscape, I think we let them close off memory as an ongoing process. We visit such memorials at our leisure and then leave them and remembering behind when we exit their spaces. I also worry that even well-intended memorial-work—the competitions, the debates, the absorption in memory—might come to substitute itself for action in light of real and ongoing persecutions. In the disappearing memorial in Hamburg-Harburg [_Monument against Fascism_], the artists suggested that monuments don’t rise up against injustice—only we can rise up against injustice. When their monument finally disappeared, they hoped, the burden of memory would be returned to those who came looking for it.

_**You have participated in discussions and decisions about several different memorial projects—the**_**Memorial to Europe’s Murdered Jews** _**, the Argentinian government’s memorial to the**_**desaparecidos** _**, and the**_**World Trade Center Memorial** _**. Have you found striking cultural differences and/or surprising points of commonality in these discussions across several continents?**_

These are all very different processes, by different groups attempting to remember toward very different ends. Some are victim groups trying to create spaces to mourn; others are governments trying to atone for the crimes of earlier regimes (and thereby distancing themselves from these earlier regimes). In the case of the _World Trade Center Memorial_ , it was clear that the city would need to regenerate the devastated area of the attacks on lower Manhattan on a “memorial-foundation.” Hence, the Lower Manhattan Development Corporation’s motto: Remember, Rebuild, Renew. This said, I think the larger, national meanings of the 9/11 attacks are still in play, even as the families of the victims have very concrete losses to mourn and remember.

**_In_ The Texture of Memory _, you say you are trying to “save our icons of remembrance from hardening into idols of remembrance.”_ _3_ 3xYoung 14. _Could you say more about this?_**

Insofar as memorials seem to remember just about everything except their own coming into being, I’ve attempted to restore a little of their own histories and origins. Who makes them and why, how they reflect a particular community’s ethos or a particular aesthetic moment, how they are received by the communities or rejected by them, how their spaces and meanings evolve over time, how they may even be destroyed—all these parts of the memorial’s performance might now come to be regarded as constituting the life of memory in these sites, what I like to call “the stages of memory.”

_**Could you say more about what you call the “culture of competing catastrophes” and the ways the past is used in this culture?**_

All kinds of groups—local, national, religious—have made as part of what unites them the vicarious memory of catastrophe. By remembering past destructions as if they happened to them directly, American groups as diverse as Jewish-Americans, African- Americans, and Native Americans have made central to their identities the remembrance of past destructions. Even though such remembrance may well seem to unify groups by giving them a seemingly “common experience,” I do worry that it generates what I’ve called a “culture of competing catastrophes,” whereby claims to moral authority and political leverage are somehow based disproportionately in the measure of remembered suffering. I also think that to some extent, we’ve begun to overcome this uni-dimensional approach to group identity, with museums and group narratives now striving to include all aspects of these groups’ experiences, looking at the rich duration of life and not just its endpoint.

_**And now for a question completely off topic: In the acknowledgments to your book**_**The Texture of Memory** _**, you thank Geoffrey Hartman for teaching you that “in writing lucidly, we humanize our scholarship.” What do you mean by this?**_

While it’s true that difficult and complex ideas and scholarship often demand difficult and complex writing, I think it’s also important that scholars find ways to share their work as broadly as possible, as a way to keep it grounded in our ongoing human lives. Whether Geoffrey knew he was teaching me this or not, I don’t know, but I did learn this from him and by his example, a long time ago.
