## 1. Where’s the Thrill in the Enhanced Games?

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** Winter 2026
**Word Count:** 3424
**Relevance Score:** 0.874

### Summary

The article examines the proposed "Enhanced Games," a Silicon Valley-backed alternative to the Olympics that would allow athletes to use performance-enhancing drugs under monitored rules. It frames the central tension as a shift from celebrating what humans can do through training and grit to showcasing what "tech titans" and medical-scientific systems can do *to* humans, turning sport into a controlled arena for legalized doping. Using analogies from Beowulf and debates about the meaning of sport, the piece argues that doped competition changes what records represent and undermines the interpersonal nature of athletic rivalry. It discusses how "fairness" is managed through equalized conditions--contraceiving dangers like doping escalation through enforcement, commissions, and approved limits--while also questioning the legitimacy of record-breaking when drug use changes the parameters of performance. The article further explores how money and incentives distort sport, parallels Silicon Valley's broader culture of biochemical "optimization," and concludes by warning that if enhancement becomes widespread, the very concept of peer-based competition may erode. It ends by suggesting an unsettling future where medicalized pills become routine for strength, joy, and bodily function, replacing the shared human struggle that sports traditionally express.

## 2. How to Think About Our Steroid Supermen

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** Spring 2008
**Word Count:** 3074
**Relevance Score:** 0.852

### Summary

The article examines the meaning of athletic achievement in an era marked by widespread use of performance-enhancing drugs. It opens with examples from the early 2000s "supermen" of sport--Lance Armstrong, Barry Bonds, and Roger Clemens--describing their dominant performances and major awards, while then contrasting how later revelations and testing regimes altered public perception. It discusses how doping investigations led to suspensions and exclusions, including the denial of Hall of Fame "first-ballot" status for Bonds and Clemens, and it notes complications in enforcement such as procedural loopholes. From there, the article frames the central question as moral rather than merely statistical: how should athletes and institutions judge and remember players who used steroids? It evaluates arguments that doping was "normalized" and therefore not uniquely unfair in a tainted era, comparing the logic to other rule or equipment changes across baseball history. Against this, it argues that the deeper problem is not only safety or legality, but the distinction between "human excellence" earned through disciplined training and a technologically altered form of performance. The piece contends that doping transforms the athlete's character and the nature of what spectators are celebrating, turning sports into spectacle rather than genuine sport. Ultimately, it argues those athletes should not be honored as "sportsmen," because they chose "supermen" created through chemical enhancement instead.

## 3. Sporting Types

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Spring 2005
**Issue:** Celebrity Culture
**Section:** Book Reviews
**Word Count:** 2234
**Relevance Score:** 0.776

### Summary

The article reviews two scholarly works about sports celebrity and its cultural effects: *Sport Stars: The Cultural Politics of Sporting Celebrity* (an edited anthology by Andrews and Jackson) and Garry Whannel's *Media Sport Stars: Masculinities and Moralities*. It centers on questions about how athletes become public figures through media and consumer capitalism, and whether sports stardom can function as a vehicle for social change. In *Sport Stars*, the main claim is that sports celebrity is deeply embedded in the economics and ideologies of consumer capitalism. Athletes are treated as both products (constructed images promoted by institutions and media) and processes (continually produced and marketed). The anthology examines how "meritocratic" narratives, competitive zero-sum rankings, and the appearance of "real-time" authenticity help turn sports stars into figures whose success supports dominant stories--especially regarding race, family values, hard work, and color-blindness. Case studies include Tiger Woods, Michael Jordan, and Dennis Rodman, showing how seemingly different star personas still reinforce particular ideological scripts. The review then highlights Whannel's focus on how sports media link celebrities to masculinity and morality. It discusses concepts such as media "vortextuality" and the way postmodern shifts destabilize moral authority while keeping athletes positioned as heroic figures, contributing to the marginalization of the feminine. Together, the books are presented as frameworks for understanding how sports celebrity shapes public culture and ideology through media representation.

## 4. Our Asterisked Heroes

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** Summer 2004
**Word Count:** 4162
**Relevance Score:** 0.760

### Summary

The article argues that heroism in sports--and more broadly in human life--may be undermined by performance enhancement, especially genetic engineering and drug-based technologies. It begins with the 1961 "asterisk" controversy in baseball: Commissioner Ford Frick ordered that Roger Maris's single-season home run record be marked with an asterisk to indicate it was not comparable to Babe Ruth's due to differences in season length and fairness of comparison. Even though the asterisk was never officially implemented, the essay treats it as a symbol of diminished or "attenuated" heroism. The piece claims baseball uniquely embodies the drama of ordinary life and that true heroism lies in accomplishments that appear improbable--moments that could have failed. It contrasts this with a future where science can predict and engineer athletic ability, reducing uncertainty. Since heroism depends on the possibility of failure and on achievements that seem more-than-human, altering genes and overriding natural risk would make feats feel "of course," thereby weakening their moral and emotional power. From sports, the argument expands to war, ethics, responsibility, and cultural meaning: if fear, courage, and capability can be chemically or genetically designed, then victories may become less admirable. The essay concludes that although enhancement will bring benefits, future societies may place an asterisk on achievements and struggle to recover the lightning-like uncertainty that makes heroism human.

## 5. The Body at Devotion

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Summer 2001
**Issue:** The Body and Being Human
**Section:** Reviews
**Word Count:** 2526
**Relevance Score:** 0.425

### Summary

The article is a review of Sarah Coakley's edited volume *Religion and the Body*, which examines how religious traditions understand, shape, and are expressed through the human body. It begins by noting common scholarly stereotypes that treat particular religions as more "embodied" than others, then argues that the collection replaces such clichés with historically grounded analysis and careful, cautious comparison. The review situates the subject within broader modern conditions: in secular societies, bodily preoccupations increase through sports, dieting, exercise regimes, health anxieties, plastic surgery, bodybuilding, and sex therapies, while eating disorders and longevity fears reflect deeper questions about discipline, transgression, and atonement-like practices. Coakley's introduction and several foundational essays explore what "body" means as an analytical category and how it relates to power, knowledge, gender, social symbols, and religious experience. The volume then surveys many traditions through bodily practices and ideas, including Jewish worship, Greek and Western Christian contexts, Reformed Protestant theology, Zoroastrianism, Hinduism, and Buddhist traditions, along with Taoism, Shinto and Japanese Buddhism, Sufism, and Sikhism. The review emphasizes that religions represent not only belief reform but "re-formations" of embodiment, while also raising concerns about the comparative framework of "religion" itself.
