## 1. Youth and Prescription Drugs

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Summer 2013
**Issue:** The American Dream
**Section:** Short Takes
**Word Count:** 640
**Relevance Score:** 0.273

### Summary

The article examines the growing use of prescription drugs among young people, centering on attention deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) diagnoses and stimulant medication. It traces public concern about medicating children to a 1970 controversy over "behavior modification" drugs such as Ritalin and Dexedrine, and then describes more recent increases in ADHD prevalence. Using Centers for Disease Control and Prevention data, it reports that in 2012 about 11% of U.S. children ages 4-17 had been diagnosed with ADHD at some point, up from 9.5% in 2007, with especially high rates among high school-age boys. Among children with current ADHD, roughly two-thirds were taking medication, typically stimulants. The article also addresses non-prescribed use of stimulants, including their reported use as "study aids" in high school and college, alongside worries about side effects and the pressure to achieve good grades. To provide context, it cites two college student surveys from 2012: the American Freshman Survey (showing large shares reporting they felt overwhelmed in high school and rising levels over time) and the National College Health Assessment (showing high rates of overwhelm, exhaustion, anxiety, loneliness, sadness, and hopelessness in college). It further reports that many students have received mental health treatment and that a substantial minority used prescription drugs without a prescription, with stimulants and painkillers among the most common.

## 2. Psychotropic Medication Use in U.S. Children and Adolescents

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Fall 2012
**Issue:** Work and Dignity
**Section:** Interviews
**Word Count:** 743
**Relevance Score:** 0.235

### Summary

The article examines the rising use of psychotropic medications among U.S. children and adolescents and the concerns associated with that trend. It notes that before 1987, these drugs were prescribed infrequently, mainly for ADHD, but that since then their use has increased substantially and the variety of conditions treated has expanded. While medication for severe, persistent mental illnesses in older adolescents and young adults is largely accepted, the article argues that increased prescribing to much younger children is more troubling because many medications were tested primarily on older populations and were previously used for diagnoses associated with later ages. A major focus is how medication patterns differ by who the patients are and how they access care. Studies cited show higher prevalence rates and faster growth among disadvantaged youth, especially those insured through Medicaid, compared with privately insured peers. Medicaid patients are also more likely to receive more powerful medications such as lithium and antipsychotics. The article then outlines key determinants of psychotropic use, highlighting patterns by age (rising use from birth through early adolescence), gender (historically more use among boys due to ADHD and stimulants, with later growth in antidepressant prescribing), and race/ethnicity (higher prescription rates among white youth than among African American and Hispanic youth).

## 3. Losing Control of ADHD

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Summer 2014
**Issue:** Minding Our Minds
**Section:** Notes And Comments
**Word Count:** 1064
**Relevance Score:** 0.233

### Summary

The article traces how ADHD became a widely diagnosed condition and argues that this expansion reflects a loss of control over the diagnostic category rather than purely medical progress. It begins with the late 1950s, when pharmaceutical companies marketed amphetamine-based stimulants for limited indications (e.g., narcolepsy, depression, weight loss, low energy). Drug interest shifted toward childhood behavior problems labeled "hyperkinetic impulse disorder," "minimal brain damage," and later "Hyperkinetic Reaction of Childhood" in the DSM. The syndrome was initially associated mainly with impulsiveness and excess motor activity in a relatively small group of children. The article then describes a turning point: in 1970 and especially with the DSM changes that linked "attention deficits" to hyperactivity. The resulting broader diagnosis--"attention deficit disorder, with or without hyperactivity" (later ADHD)--made hyperactivity optional and expanded the number of potential cases. As diagnostic criteria and treatment practices evolved, the prevalence rose dramatically, accompanied by large-scale stimulant use. It highlights CDC survey findings of ADHD diagnoses in a substantial share of children and a sizable number currently taking stimulant medication. Finally, it challenges claims that rising rates represent narrowing the gap to "true prevalence," arguing that epidemiological "cases" based on symptom reports and DSM thresholds are not the same as clinician-confirmed mental disorders.

## 4. Patterns among Leading Drug Classes

**Publication:** hedgehog-review
**Date:** Fall 2012
**Issue:** Work and Dignity
**Section:** Interviews
**Word Count:** 933
**Relevance Score:** 0.223

### Summary

The article examines patterns in the use of major psychotropic medication classes for U.S. children and adolescents over roughly the past two decades, focusing on evidence of benefit, safety concerns, and changes in prescribing in community settings. It highlights stimulants first, noting that the strongest youth-treatment evidence exists for stimulants based on a controlled study of young children, but follow-up shows early symptom-control gains were not sustained long-term compared with community controls. Despite this, stimulant prescribing has increased modestly, with variation by region and higher use in some Medicaid populations. Next, it discusses antidepressants, emphasizing that use expanded substantially with SSRIs/SNRIs such as fluoxetine. It describes emerging concerns about suicidality in adults and later in youth, leading to FDA hearings and a boxed warning. After this warning, antidepressant use declined, though it did not clearly decrease for youth diagnosed with major depression, suggesting prescribing may have reflected severity. Alternatives involving combined drug-plus-therapy approaches in primary care are also mentioned. The article then addresses antipsychotics, noting substantial increases since the 1980s, especially among Medicaid-insured youth. It questions appropriateness for behavioral conditions in young children and stresses documented risks from second-generation antipsychotics, including weight gain, metabolic abnormalities, and elevated prolactin, with limited long-term pediatric safety evidence. Finally, it notes rising use of concurrent medication class regimens, despite limited clinical-trial evidence and potential for broader adverse-event exposure.

## 5. The Downer About Uppers

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** Fall 2023
**Word Count:** 4213
**Relevance Score:** 0.181

### Summary

The article examines the recent resurgence of amphetamine use in the United States, linking a shortage and soaring prescriptions for prescription amphetamines like Adderall to a broader national pattern of stimulant dependence and misuse alongside the ongoing methamphetamine overdose crisis. It describes how Adderall demand surged--driven in part by pandemic-era telehealth prescribing--while supply remains constrained because amphetamine is a controlled substance produced in limited quantities. Patients report rationing medication or traveling to obtain it. At the same time, the article highlights methamphetamine's rapid growth in availability and affordability, noting a steep increase in overdose deaths where psychostimulants, especially meth, play a major role. It explains that Adderall and meth are chemically related as amphetamines and provides historical context: early widespread use of amphetamine medications in the mid-20th century, followed by rising recognition of addiction, psychosis, and other harms, and then regulatory tightening. The article then traces the mid-1990s return of both medical amphetamine (with rising ADHD diagnoses and approval of Adderall for ADHD) and illicit meth production, shaped by changing supply chains and enforcement. Finally, it argues that licit and illicit amphetamine problems share underlying cultural and market dynamics--treating stimulants as productivity "helpers" and enabling both therapeutic and destructive uses--suggesting repeating cycles of experimentation, harm, and backlash.
