# Psychotropic Medication Use in U.S. Children and Adolescents

Before 1987, psychotropic drugs were rarely prescribed for children. When they were, it was largely to treat Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD). Since that time psychotropic drug use for children and teenagers has grown two to five-fold, and the range of problems for which they are prescribed has widened considerably. The increase of drug treatment has prompted concern from the lay public, investigative journalists, and indeed some health professionals. This concern should not be mistaken for blanket skepticism of all pediatric psychiatry: the drug treatment of severe and persistent mental illness (such as schizophrenia, obsessive compulsive disorder, or bipolar disorder) in late adolescence and early adulthood has been largely accepted. But when very young children receive diagnoses formerly reserved for late adolescence and adulthood, and when the drugs treating their diagnosed conditions have primarily been tested on older persons, it is not surprising that the rapid growth in psychotropic medication use in the young is met with concern and skepticism.

The question of prescribing psychotropic medication to children becomes potentially more worrisome when we ask whose children are being medicated, and with what medications. While all studies show the use of these drugs rising steadily, studies focusing on disadvantaged children and teens reveal a higher prevalence, and sometimes also a faster rate of increase, than is seen in their wealthier peers. In one study, for example, privately insured fourteen-to eighteen-year-olds showed an increase in prevalence rates from 3.4 percent in 1997 to 8.3 percent in 2001; comparable rates for youth fifteen-to nineteen-years-old with Medicaid coverage were 8.3 percent in 1997 and 11.9 percent in 2001. In another case, one state Medicaid-insured program showed a ten- fold growth in psychotropic prescriptions within thirteen years (1990–2003) among the youngest children. Even when total use is comparable for children in these insurance groups, the drugs prescribed are not: those dependent on public insurance are disproportionately likely to receive the most powerful, and potentially the most dangerous, drugs. A ten-year study comparing Medicaid- and HMO-enrollee data from 1987 to 1996 showed that in 1996, lithium use was two to four times higher, and antipsychotic use five to eight times higher, in Medicaid than HMO patients. Empirical testing of the notion that the insurance-type disparities are due to greater severity in the Medicaid-insured population is needed, especially for youth.

In summary, the past two decades have produced dramatic growth in the use of psychotropic drugs in all community treatment settings, with substantially greater rates among Medicaid-insured than in privately insured youth. These relatively large increases in numbers of youth prescribed a psychotropic drug in a given year can be interpreted quite differently depending on one’s position among the various stakeholders, for example, parent, child, neighbor, teacher, physician, researcher, or policy maker. Interestingly, total prevalence data do not reveal the impact of key social and clinical determinants; these distinctions will now be examined.

## _K_ _e_ _y Determinants of Psychotropic Medication Use_

When total psychotropic medication use is stratified by key determinants, various patterns are revealed. Three leading determinants in one mid-Atlantic state program are detailed below (see table).

Age Group. Medicaid data stratified by age group show a linear increase from birth through age fourteen with a lower usage in late adolescence. This pattern is contrary to the expected use relative to the age of onset of schizophrenia and other serious mental diagnoses and suggests that youth with problems in living are presenting themselves to the medical arena and are receiving treatment with psychotropic medications at younger ages and for behavioral conditions that may not have been presented as medical conditions in the past.

Gender. Boys have typically dominated pediatric psychotropic medication use because they are the primary recipients of ADHD diagnoses and subsequent treatment with stimulants. In the late 1980s, male to female ratios were typically around 6:1. While stimulants still comprise the bulk of psychotropic use in children, since the early 1990s antidepressant use has also been rising.

Race/Ethnicity. Reducing disparities in access to health services in relation to race/ethnicity has been a major national priority for the past two decades. State Medicaid data include self-identified race/ethnicity in the enrollment files, which permits use of psychotropic drugs to be categorized accordingly. We can see that in this mid-Atlantic state Medicaid program, white youth were twice as likely as their African American peers to have had prescriptions for psychotropic medication. The disparity (greater usage in white youth) was even greater when comparing their use to Hispanic youth.
