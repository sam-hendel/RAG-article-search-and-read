# Patterns among Leading Drug Classes

## _S_ _timulants_

The best evidence supporting youth treatment with a psychotropic drug class is found for stimulants. A large, publicly funded study of seven-to ten-year-olds established a significant benefit of stimulant treatment using a defined treatment protocol over a fourteen-month period. On the other hand, follow-up data at intervals up to eight years reveal that the early gains in symptom control by those assigned to stimulant treatment were not sustained in terms of continuing use and improvement; study treatment groups at eight years did no better on numerous measures of outcome than those randomly assigned to a community control. And psychotherapy experts have criticized some of the strong inferences promulgated on the benefits of stimulant therapy alone to treat behavioral disorders.

In terms of community treatment with stimulants, more youth were receiving prescribed stimulants in 2007 than previously, representing approximately 4.8 percent of youth four-to seventeen-years-old. While the proportion of youth treated with stimulants is increasing, the rate of change is modest. for HMO-insured youth in northern California, one study showed a modest increase in stimulant use from 1.86 percent to 1.93 percent across five years, from 1996 to 2000. Use was more than twice as high but with similar growth (from 3.3 percent to 3.6 percent) in Vermont Medicaid-insured youth observed from 1997 to 2007. It should be noted that analyses showing the increased use of medications are complicated by the baseline prevalence, since change from lower baselines can show a greater degree of increase. It should also be noted that there is marked regional variation in the use of stimulants, which is lowest in the western U.S. region and highest in the southern U.S. region.

## _Antidepressants_

The second most commonly used psychotropic drug class is antidepressants (ATD), and ATD use has grown substantially since the advent of selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) and serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs). When the most famous of these, Prozac (fluoxetine), was marketed in 1988, antidepressant use across the age groups strikingly increased. This increase was viewed as a benefit of newer products as they lacked the undesirable anticholinergic adverse effects that patients complained of with tricyclic antidepressants and that often led to their premature discontinuation. However, suspicions of increased suicidal behavior in adults emerged shortly after fluoxetine was marketed, and by the late 1990s, a similar concern emerged regarding children and adolescents treated with SSRIs. These concerns led to another round of FDA public hearings in 2004. The net result was an FDA-conducted meta-analysis of antidepressant clinical trials among youth, resulting in a boxed warning on the risk of “suicidality” (suicidal thoughts and behavior) for youth treated with antidepressants. After wide promulgation of this information in the media, the use of antidepressants was shown to decrease approximately 16 percent in the years following the FDA warning. However, when the analysis was restricted to youth with a diagnosis of major depression, there was no significant change in use pre-to post-FDA warning—suggesting that physicians were treating appropriately, that is, according to the level of severity. Other alternatives to widespread ATD use are suggested by an effectiveness study that showed the benefits of short-term integrated treatment interventions (drug plus psychotherapy) in primary care for adolescents, leading to a reduced likelihood of severe depression after six months. In terms of overall benefits, antidepressant evidence for several SSRIs is supported for adolescents but not for children.

## _A_ _ntipsychotics_

Antipsychotic drug prevalence in youth has risen substantially since the 1980s. Recent data from a seven-state Medicaid dataset reveals that antipsychotic use rose from 2.7 percent in 2001 to 4.2 percent in 2004 among six-to seventeen-year-olds. National data corroborate the Medicaid data, but show a much lower exposure to antipsychotics in privately insured than Medicaid-insured youth. Still, national physician office visit survey data from 1993 to 2002 showed that visits to physicians involving a prescribed antipsychotic for youths increased fourfold across all types of insurance in a decade. This increase exceeds the expected prevalence if prescribing were restricted to the serious—but low prevalence—conditions with indications for antipsychotic use, for example, schizophrenia and severe developmental disorders. These data for the second generation antipsychotics pose serious questions regarding the appropriateness of such drugs to treat behavioral conditions, particularly in very young children.

Indeed, perhaps the most jarring news on psychotropic patterns in youth concerns the substantial risks documented for the second generation antipsychotics in community popu- lations. These studies conducted on community populations in psychiatric clinic settings reveal potentially serious adverse side effects: elevated prolactin levels, weight gain and abnormal metabolic measures related to increased liver enzymes, increased blood lipid levels, and higher glucose levels. These data challenge long-term pediatric antipsychotic safety, of which there is inadequate study.

## _C_ _oncomitant Drug Class Regimens_

The discussion so far has only considered monotherapy drug usage; in fact the use of more than one class of drug, simultaneously co-prescribed, is also increasing. yet combined therapy has little research supportive evidence of benefit/risk under clinical trial conditions, and no outcomes in terms of benefit/risk in community- treated populations. Combinations of drugs can increase the risk of exposure to a wider range of adverse events than are likely with less complex regimens.

In summary, this brief overview of quantita- tive changes shows that the role of psychotropic medications for the treatment of emotional and behavioral disorders in U.S. children and adolescents during the past twenty years has increased many-fold, particularly among publicly insured, socially vulnerable, and economically disadvantaged youth. These descriptive patterns of increased use include very young children, for whom serious concerns of appropriateness and safety have been raised by academic researchers in child psychiatry.
