## 1. The AI Genetics Revolution Is Coming

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** Fall 2025
**Word Count:** 3203
**Relevance Score:** 0.817

### Summary

The article argues that the most urgent AI-related danger may not be superintelligent, runaway systems, but an "AI genetics revolution" that accelerates gene editing and could deepen social inequality. It describes how modern AI--especially large language models--can read, interpret, and even generate biological "genetic language," potentially making it far easier to predict how combinations of mutations cause disease and to design new genetic sequences. It highlights emerging projects such as AI systems trained on enormous genomic datasets, AI approaches to generate 3D face images from DNA traces, and AI-assisted methods for creating synthetic DNA that can control gene activity. It also explains how AI can improve CRISPR gene editing by designing more precise editing tools and reducing off-target effects. The piece emphasizes that these capabilities are medically promising (better therapies, personalized medicine), but ethically perilous, particularly when paired with technologies like in vitro gametogenesis that could enable large-scale embryo creation and selection. It links this to eugenics "by other means," raising questions about the boundary between therapy and enhancement. A major focus is geopolitics: the article claims China is positioned to lead in applying these technologies, potentially adopting different ethical standards, exploiting genomic data access, and tolerating more ethically controversial experiments--creating a race the United States may struggle to counter.

## 2. Gene Editing: New Technology,  Old Moral Questions

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** Winter 2016
**Word Count:** 5577
**Relevance Score:** 0.923

### Summary

The article examines how CRISPR-Cas9 gene-editing could transform medicine and human reproduction, while also reopening long-standing ethical controversies. It first describes CRISPR's practical appeal: RNA-guided enzymes can be programmed to cut targeted DNA sites, making genome targeting more accurate and flexible than earlier methods. The piece surveys current and near-term limitations, arguing that gene therapy will likely remain constrained by the imperfect state of clinical genetics--since diagnosing and predicting many conditions from genetic information alone is still error-prone, and complex traits like intelligence or athleticism have poorly understood genetic underpinnings. From there, it focuses on ethics, especially the distinction between somatic editing (changes not inherited by offspring) and germline editing (changes passed to descendants). It challenges the simplicity of that "bright line," noting that editing embryos can function both as treatment and as unintended germline modification. It also discusses consent, arguing that the real moral question is whether and how future generations' well-being should factor into decisions for current patients. Finally, the article connects contemporary debates to the historical legacy of eugenics, warns against valuing abstract notions like the "germline" over treating actual patients, and critiques embryo-destruction approaches that would avoid germline spread. It argues for a pro-life case for therapeutic embryo gene editing to prevent lethal or early-manifesting disease, while expressing skepticism about "enhancement" aimed at designing preferred traits in children.

## 3. Take No for an Answer on Genetic Engineering

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** Summer 2023
**Word Count:** 2471
**Relevance Score:** 0.892

### Summary

The article focuses on the ethical and political dispute over CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing, especially whether it should be used on human embryos in ways that would pass changes to future generations ("heritable" or germline editing). It notes that CRISPR has moved from theoretical concern to real medical progress: in 2023, companies entered the FDA approval process for gene therapies aimed at treating sickle cell disease and beta thalassemia. Those therapies modify somatic cells rather than being inherited, but the article argues that the same technology could eventually be repurposed for embryo editing, including "enhancement" goals such as appearance or "competitive" traits, raising fears of "designer babies," eugenics, and a dystopian future. It contrasts two approaches. Mainstream CRISPR researchers and ethicists have held international summits to develop ethical boundaries and seek broad societal consensus, producing cautious, conditional statements rather than clear permission. Skeptics influenced by bioethicists like Leon Kass and Paul Ramsey argue that germline engineering should be prohibited outright, emphasizing "caution, restraint, delay, abstention." Finally, the article claims that policy has already settled the issue: many countries--including the United States--have laws restricting or banning heritable embryo editing. In the US, it describes a congressional appropriations rider effectively preventing the FDA from considering approvals for germline-editing trials, arguing that democracy has already delivered a "no."

## 4. Human Gene Editing Arrives in America

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** Winter 2016
**Word Count:** 917
**Relevance Score:** 0.863

### Summary

The article discusses a report that scientists in the United States have genetically edited human embryos for the first time using CRISPR, with greater precision than earlier attempts. It focuses on work by Shoukhrat Mitalipov's team at Oregon Health and Science University, who was previously involved in creating usable stem cells from cloned human embryos. Although the results have not yet been published, the article says the team reportedly corrected problems seen in a prior Chinese announcement, including errors introduced by CRISPR and incomplete editing in some cells. A key distinction is the type of embryos used. The earlier Chinese work relied on non-viable "tripronuclear" embryos discarded from IVF clinics, while the American approach created embryos expressly for experimentation by injecting CRISPR into egg cells at fertilization. The article describes the intended purpose as proof-of-principle for preventing genetic disease, specifically beta thalassemia, by editing embryos fathered by men with the recessive disorder. It argues that despite disease prevention as the stated justification, alternatives like preimplantation genetic diagnosis (PGD) already exist and are cheaper and more established, while critique centers on the ethical acceptability of destroying embryos created for research. The discussion broadens to concerns that such experiments contribute to the instrumentalization and disposal of human life in laboratories and that enhancement uses of gene editing may pose additional ethical challenges.

## 5. First Thoughts on Germline Engineering

**Publication:** new-atlantis
**Date:** 2020
**Word Count:** 1143
**Relevance Score:** 0.838

### Summary

The article discusses the announcement that scientists in China genetically modified human embryos using CRISPR, targeting the gene associated with beta thalassemia. The embryos were not affected by the disease, and the researchers used abnormally fertilized "tripronuclear" embryos--fertilized by two sperm so they contain three sets of chromosomes--to avoid directly experimenting on embryos meant for normal development. The piece argues that the scientific and ethical concerns raised by this work are heightened because the modifications were carried out with very poor accuracy: many embryos had off-target edits, and many developed as genetic mosaics, making it impossible to reliably verify the intended changes using approaches such as preimplantation genetic diagnosis. The main focus is that this research crosses a major bioethical taboo by altering the human germline, meaning the changes could be inherited by future generations. The article describes prior animal and cell research that made this step foreseeable and notes responses from scientists and ethicists, including recommendations to strongly discourage clinical germline modification and calls for a voluntary moratorium. It also contrasts germline editing with existing reproductive practices like embryo selection and PGD, questioning how both currently accepted and new technologies shape the meaning of human procreation.
